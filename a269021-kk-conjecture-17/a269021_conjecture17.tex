\begin{abstract}
\noindent Let $a(n)$ be the number of permutations of $\{1,\dots,2n\}$ that contain an increasing
subsequence of length $n$ (OEIS \OEIS{A269021}). From $42$ known terms, Kauers and Koutschan
guessed a linear recurrence for $a(n)$ of order $4$ with polynomial coefficients of degree $21$,
and listed it as an open conjecture (their Conjecture~17): no proof was available because no
finite, a~priori representation of $a(n)$ was known --- the classical D-finiteness results for
pattern containment fix the pattern length, while here the pattern length $n$ is coupled to the
permutation length $2n$. We prove the conjectured recurrence. The key is the exact representation
\[
  a(n) \;=\; (2n)!^{2}\sum_{\rho=n}^{2n}
  \frac{(-1)^{\rho-n}\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-n}}{(\rho!)^{2}\,(2n-\rho)!}\,,
\]
which follows from the Robinson--Schensted correspondence, the Borodin--Okounkov--Olshanski
formula for the one-point correlation function of the poissonized Plancherel measure (the discrete
Bessel kernel), and the elementary observation that a partition of $2n$ has at most one row of
length at least $n$, so the relevant inclusion--exclusion truncates after one term. The summand is
a proper hypergeometric term vanishing outside $n\le\rho\le 2n$, and Zeilberger's algorithm
returns as its minimal telescoper \emph{exactly} the Kauers--Koutschan operator; we exhibit the
rational certificate and prove the two resulting rational-function identities deterministically
(exact evaluation on integer grids exceeding explicit degree bounds). As corollaries,
$a(n)/(2n)!^{2}$ is P-recursive --- to our knowledge the first D-finiteness proof for this
coupled diagonal --- and
the same method gives a single-sum formula for the number of permutations of $[2n]$ whose longest
increasing subsequence has any prescribed length $\ge n$. All computations are reproducible from
one self-contained script.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In their automated study of possibly-D-finite sequences in the OEIS, Kauers and
Koutschan~\cite{KK23} examined the sequence \OEIS{A269021},
\[
  a(n)\;=\;\#\{\sigma\in S_{2n}\ :\ \sigma\text{ contains the pattern }12\cdots n\}
       \;=\;\#\{\sigma\in S_{2n} : \LIS(\sigma)\ge n\},
\]
where $\LIS$ denotes the length of the longest increasing subsequence:
\[
  1,\ 2,\ 23,\ 588,\ 24553,\ 1438112,\ 108469917,\ \dots\qquad(n\ge 0).
\]
From the $42$ terms then in the OEIS they detected a linear recurrence of order $4$ and degree
$21$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Lrec}
  c_0(n)\,a(n)+c_1(n)\,a(n{+}1)+c_2(n)\,a(n{+}2)+c_3(n)\,a(n{+}3)+c_4(n)\,a(n{+}4)\;=\;0,
\end{equation}
with explicit $c_i\in\ZZ[n]$, $\deg c_i\le 21$ (the coefficients are reproduced in
Appendix~\ref{app:rec}); equivalently, in the normalization $\tilde a_n=a(n)/(2n)!^{2}$ used in
their paper, this is their Conjecture~17. They marked the case \textbf{O} --- open: neither the
recurrence nor even the D-finiteness of the sequence was proved. They are explicit about the
obstruction~\cite[\S6]{KK23}:

\begin{callout}
``It is known [Gessel; Bostan et al.] that for every fixed $k$, the number of permutations of
length $n$ avoiding the pattern $123\cdots k$ is D-finite as a sequence in $n$. However, this
result has no immediate implications on sequences we obtain when $n$ and $k$ are coupled.''
\end{callout}

Indeed, for \emph{fixed} $k$ the counting sequence is governed by Gessel's Bessel--Toeplitz
determinant~\cite{Ges90}, but the determinant's size grows with $k$; on the coupled diagonal
$k=n$, $m=2n$ no finite a~priori representation --- a finite sum or integral of hypergeometric
type to which creative telescoping could be applied --- was on record. That missing
representation is the entire content of the present note; once it is found, the rest is the
standard Wilf--Zeilberger method, and the certificate turns out to land exactly on the guessed
operator.

\begin{theorem}\label{main}
The recurrence \eqref{eq:Lrec} holds for all integers $n\ge 0$ with the exact coefficients
$c_0,\dots,c_4$ recorded by Kauers and Koutschan. Equivalently, Conjecture~17 of \cite{KK23}
holds. In particular $\tilde a_n=a(n)/(2n)!^{2}$ is P-recursive.
\end{theorem}

The proof has two independent halves:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
\item \textbf{A proven representation (Section~\ref{sec:rep}).} We show
\begin{equation}\label{eq:star}
  a(n) \;=\; \sum_{\rho\in\ZZ} t(n,\rho),\qquad
  t(n,\rho):=\frac{(-1)^{\rho-n}\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-n}\,(2n)!^{2}}{(\rho!)^{2}\,(2n-\rho)!}\,,
\end{equation}
the summand vanishing outside $n\le\rho\le2n$. This rests on the Robinson--Schensted
correspondence, on the Borodin--Okounkov--Olshanski (BOO) formula for the one-point correlation
function of the poissonized Plancherel measure, and on a one-line combinatorial truncation: a
partition of $2n$ has at most one row of length $\ge n$.
\item \textbf{A Wilf--Zeilberger certificate (Section~\ref{sec:wz}).} The term $t(n,\rho)$ is
proper hypergeometric, and the Kauers--Koutschan operator
$\Lop=\sum_{i=0}^{4}c_i(n)N^i$ (where $N$ is the shift $n\mapsto n+1$) telescopes it: we exhibit
an explicit rational function $\Rt(n,\rho)$ with
$\Lop\,t = G(n,\rho{+}1)-G(n,\rho)$ for a closed-form $G$ built from $\Rt$, and prove the two
rational-function identities this requires by exact evaluation on integer grids exceeding
explicit degree bounds --- a deterministic, finite proof. Summing over $\rho$ then gives
$\Lop\,a=0$ for all $n\ge5$; the finitely many initial cases are checked directly from
\eqref{eq:star}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why a term-match is not a proof, and what is genuinely needed}

The $42$-term b-file cannot prove \eqref{eq:Lrec}: the recurrence has
$5\cdot22=110$ unknown coefficients, and any candidate operator fitted to data --- or any second
operator obtained from data by closure properties --- begs the question, since exhibiting
\emph{any} bounded-order annihilator of $a(n)$ is essentially equivalent to the D-finiteness
assertion being proved. (Kauers and Koutschan of course knew this; it is why they published the
recurrence as a conjecture.) What breaks the circle is a representation of $a(n)$ that is proved
\emph{before} any recurrence enters: \eqref{eq:star} is derived from published theorems with no
reference to \eqref{eq:Lrec}, and the conjectured operator then enters only as the \emph{candidate
telescoper} whose certificate is verified symbolically. This is the standard architecture of a
Wilf--Zeilberger proof~\cite{Zei91,PWZ96}, and it is the exact route Kauers--Koutschan use for
the conjectures in their paper that they \emph{could} prove --- what was missing for
\OEIS{A269021} was only the representation.

\section{The representation}\label{sec:rep}

Throughout, $\lambda=(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots)$ is a partition, $|\lambda|$ its size,
$f^\lambda$ the number of standard Young tableaux of shape $\lambda$.

\subsection{Ingredients}

\paragraph{(i) Robinson--Schensted.} $\LIS(\sigma)=\lambda_1(\mathrm{RS}(\sigma))$ and the
correspondence is a bijection $S_m\to\bigcup_{\lambda\vdash m}\mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)^2$
\cite{Sch61}, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rsk}
  a(n)\;=\;\sum_{\substack{\lambda\vdash 2n\\ \lambda_1\ge n}} (f^\lambda)^2 .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(ii) The one-row truncation.} For a partition $\lambda$ define its \emph{descent set}
\[
  \Dset(\lambda)\;=\;\{\lambda_i-i\ :\ i\ge1\}\ \subset\ \ZZ
\]
(the convention of \cite[\S1.2]{BOO00}; the map $i\mapsto\lambda_i-i$ is strictly decreasing, so
each value is attained at most once).

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:trunc}
Let $\lambda\vdash 2n$ and $x\ge n-1$ an integer. If $x\in\Dset(\lambda)$, i.e.\
$x=\lambda_i-i$ for some $i$, then $i=1$. Consequently
\[
  \#\bigl(\Dset(\lambda)\cap\ZZ_{\ge n-1}\bigr)\;=\;
  \begin{cases} 1, & \lambda_1\ge n \quad(\text{the element } \lambda_1-1),\\
                0, & \lambda_1\le n-1. \end{cases}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $\lambda_i-i=x\ge n-1$ with $i\ge2$ then $\lambda_2\ge\lambda_i=x+i\ge n+1$, so
$|\lambda|\ge\lambda_1+\lambda_2\ge 2\lambda_2\ge 2n+2>2n$, a contradiction. For $i=1$,
$\lambda_1-1\ge n-1\iff\lambda_1\ge n$.
\end{proof}

Combining with \eqref{eq:rsk},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:count}
  a(n)\;=\;\sum_{\lambda\vdash 2n}(f^\lambda)^2\cdot
           \#\bigl(\Dset(\lambda)\cap\ZZ_{\ge n-1}\bigr).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(iii) The BOO one-point function.} For $\theta>0$ the \emph{poissonized Plancherel
measure} on the set of all partitions is
\[
  M^\theta(\lambda)\;=\;e^{-\theta}\,\theta^{|\lambda|}\Bigl(\frac{f^\lambda}{|\lambda|!}\Bigr)^{2}.
\]
Borodin, Okounkov and Olshanski prove (\cite{BOO00}, the second theorem of their introduction
--- Theorem~2 in the arXiv numbering) that the correlation functions of $\Dset(\lambda)$ under
$M^\theta$ are determinantal with the \emph{discrete Bessel kernel}; we need only the one-point
case: for every $x\in\ZZ$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:boo}
  M^\theta\bigl(\{\lambda : x\in\Dset(\lambda)\}\bigr)\;=\;\JJ(x,x;\theta),
  \qquad
  \JJ(x,y;\theta)=\sqrt{\theta}\,\frac{J_x J_{y+1}-J_{x+1}J_y}{x-y},
\end{equation}
where $J_x=J_x(2\sqrt\theta)$ is the Bessel function (the diagonal is the usual limit). We use
the denominator-free series form (\cite{BOO00}, Proposition~2.6, eq.~(2.14) in the arXiv v2
numbering):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernelseries}
  \JJ(x,y;\theta)\;=\;\sum_{m\ge0}(-1)^m\,
  \frac{(x+y+m+2)_m}{\Gamma(x+m+2)\,\Gamma(y+m+2)}\cdot\frac{\theta^{\frac{x+y}{2}+m+1}}{m!}\,,
\end{equation}
where $(z)_m=z(z{+}1)\cdots(z{+}m{-}1)$ denotes the rising factorial. (An equivalent
expansion $\JJ(x,y)=\sum_{s\ge1}J_{x+s}J_{y+s}$ is also derived there; we will not
need it.)

\subsection{The formula}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:rep}
For all $n\ge1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:staragain}
  a(n) \;=\; (2n)!^{2}\sum_{\rho=n}^{2n}
  \frac{(-1)^{\rho-n}\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-n}}{(\rho!)^{2}\,(2n-\rho)!}\,,
\end{equation}
and, refining by the exact value of the longest increasing subsequence, for every $j\ge0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:starprime}
  \#\{\sigma\in S_{2n} : \LIS(\sigma)=n+j\}
  \;=\;(2n)!^{2}\sum_{\rho=n+j}^{2n}
  \frac{(-1)^{\rho-n-j}\binom{2\rho-1}{\rho-n-j}}{(\rho!)^{2}\,(2n-\rho)!}\,.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix an integer $x\ge n-1$. Multiplying \eqref{eq:boo} by $e^{\theta}$ gives
\[
  \sum_{\lambda}\theta^{|\lambda|}\Bigl(\frac{f^\lambda}{|\lambda|!}\Bigr)^{2}
  \,[\,x\in\Dset(\lambda)\,]
  \;=\;e^{\theta}\,\JJ(x,x;\theta)\qquad(\theta>0).
\]
Both sides are entire functions of $\theta$: the left side is a power series whose $\theta^m$
coefficient is at most $\sum_{\lambda\vdash m}(f^\lambda/m!)^2=1/m!$, and the right side is
$e^\theta$ times the everywhere-convergent series \eqref{eq:kernelseries}. Agreement on
$(0,\infty)$ therefore forces agreement of all power-series coefficients. Extracting
$[\theta^{2n}]$ (only $|\lambda|=2n$ contributes on the left):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:onepoint2n}
  \frac{1}{(2n)!^{2}}\sum_{\lambda\vdash2n}(f^\lambda)^2\,[\,x\in\Dset(\lambda)\,]
  \;=\;[\theta^{2n}]\;e^\theta\,\JJ(x,x;\theta).
\end{equation}
On the diagonal $x=y$, with $x\ge0$ an integer, \eqref{eq:kernelseries} reads
\[
\JJ(x,x;\theta)=\sum_{m\ge0}(-1)^m\frac{(2x+m+2)_m}{((x+m+1)!)^{2}\,m!}\,\theta^{x+m+1}.
\]
Summing \eqref{eq:onepoint2n} over all integers $x\ge n-1$ and using \eqref{eq:count}:
\[
  \frac{a(n)}{(2n)!^{2}}\;=\;[\theta^{2n}]\;e^\theta \sum_{x\ge n-1}\JJ(x,x;\theta).
\]
Reindex the double series over $(x,m)$ by $\rho=x+m+1$ and $k=x+1$ (so $k$ runs over
$n\le k\le\rho$ and $m=\rho-k$). Since
$(2x+m+2)_m=(k+\rho)(k+\rho+1)\cdots(2\rho-1)=\frac{(2\rho-1)!}{(\rho+k-1)!}$ and
$(x+m+1)!=\rho!$,
\[
  \sum_{x\ge n-1}\JJ(x,x;\theta)
  \;=\;\sum_{\rho\ge n}\frac{\theta^{\rho}}{(\rho!)^{2}}
  \sum_{k=n}^{\rho}(-1)^{\rho-k}\binom{2\rho-1}{\rho-k}.
\]
The inner alternating partial sum collapses by the elementary identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:collapse}
  \sum_{d=0}^{D}(-1)^d\binom{M}{d}\;=\;(-1)^D\binom{M-1}{D}
  \qquad(M\ge1,\ D\ge0),
\end{equation}
(immediate by induction on $D$ from Pascal's rule), with $M=2\rho-1$, $d=\rho-k$, $D=\rho-n$:
\[
  \sum_{k=n}^{\rho}(-1)^{\rho-k}\binom{2\rho-1}{\rho-k}\;=\;(-1)^{\rho-n}\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-n}.
\]
Finally $[\theta^{2n}]e^{\theta}\theta^{\rho}=1/(2n-\rho)!$ (interpreted as $0$ for $\rho>2n$),
giving \eqref{eq:staragain}. For \eqref{eq:starprime}, use the single point $x=n+j-1$ in
\eqref{eq:onepoint2n} instead of the sum over $x$: by Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc},
$x\in\Dset(\lambda)\iff\lambda_1=n+j$ for $\lambda\vdash2n$, and the same reindexing (now with
$k$ fixed at $n+j$, without the collapse step) gives the stated single sum with
$\binom{2\rho-1}{\rho-n-j}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:checks}
Proposition~\ref{prop:rep} is exact, not asymptotic, and it is heavily over-verified in the
reproducibility script: \eqref{eq:staragain} agrees with a brute-force count over \emph{all}
$(2n)!$ permutations for $n\le3$, with the hook-length sum \eqref{eq:rsk} for $n\le10$, and with
the $16$ OEIS-listed terms; during the research run it was checked against $81$ independently
computed terms ($n\le80$) and \eqref{eq:starprime} against the full exact array of LIS
distributions for $n\le26$ ($494$ values), with no discrepancies.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The right side of \eqref{eq:starprime} makes sense for all $j\ge0$ and vanishes for $j>n$, as it
must. Identity \eqref{eq:starprime} is a closed single-sum formula for the upper half of the
distribution of $\LIS$ on $S_{2n}$ --- the regime where at most one row of the Plancherel shape
can be long, so the Fredholm/inclusion--exclusion expansion of the gap probability truncates at
its first term. We are not aware of it appearing in the literature, though all ingredients date
to 2000 or earlier.
\end{remark}

\section{The Wilf--Zeilberger certificate}\label{sec:wz}

Write $t(n,\rho)$ for the summand of \eqref{eq:star}, extended to all integers $\rho$ by its
natural conventions: $\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-n}=0$ for $\rho<n$ (negative lower index) and
$1/(2n-\rho)!=0$ for $\rho>2n$. Thus $t(n,\cdot)$ is supported on $[n,2n]$ and
$a(n)=\sum_{\rho\in\ZZ}t(n,\rho)$, a finite sum. Let
\[
  \Lop\;=\;c_0(n)+c_1(n)N+c_2(n)N^2+c_3(n)N^3+c_4(n)N^4
\]
be the Kauers--Koutschan operator (Appendix~\ref{app:rec}), acting on the $n$ variable, and set
\[
  (\Lop t)(n,\rho)\;=\;\sum_{i=0}^{4}c_i(n)\,t(n{+}i,\rho),
\]
a combination supported on $n\le\rho\le2n+8$.

\subsection{Discovery, and what is actually proved}

Running Zeilberger's algorithm (Maxima's \texttt{zeilberger} package~\cite{Maxima}) on the
hypergeometric term $t(n,\rho)$ returns a minimal telescoper of order $4$ --- and its
coefficients agree \emph{exactly}, polynomial for polynomial, with the guessed
$c_0,\dots,c_4$. The algorithm also returns a rational certificate. We stress the logical role
of this step: it is \emph{discovery only}. Nothing below depends on the correctness of Maxima;
the certificate is re-stated in closed form and the identities it must satisfy are proved
independently and deterministically.

\subsection{The certificate and the two identities}

All the rational functions below arise from ratios of $t$; the elementary factorial
manipulations are stated as a lemma.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ratios}
For integers $\nu,\rho$ in the indicated ranges (all denominators nonzero),
\[
  \frac{t(\nu,\rho+1)}{t(\nu,\rho)}
  =\frac{-(2\rho)(2\rho{-}1)(2\nu{-}\rho)}{(\rho{+}1{-}\nu)(\rho{+}\nu{-}1)(\rho{+}1)^2},
  \qquad
  \frac{t(\nu{+}1,\rho)}{t(\nu,\rho)}
  =\frac{-(2\nu{+}1)^2(2\nu{+}2)^2(\rho{-}\nu)}{(\rho{+}\nu{-}1)(2\nu{+}2{-}\rho)(2\nu{+}1{-}\rho)}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Direct computation from the definition of $t$; e.g.\ for the second,
$\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-\nu-1}\big/\binom{2\rho-2}{\rho-\nu}=\frac{\rho-\nu}{\rho+\nu-1}$ and
$(2\nu{+}2)!^2/(2\nu)!^2=(2\nu{+}1)^2(2\nu{+}2)^2$, while $(2\nu{-}\rho)!/(2\nu{+}2{-}\rho)!
=1/\bigl((2\nu{+}2{-}\rho)(2\nu{+}1{-}\rho)\bigr)$, and the sign alternates once. (The script
re-checks both formulas against direct factorial ratios at every interior lattice point of
several rows.)
\end{proof}

We work in the basis of the largest shift. Define, for $0\le i\le4$,
\begin{align}
  \sigma_i(n,\rho)\;&:=\;\prod_{k=i}^{3}
  \frac{-(\rho{+}n{+}k{-}1)\,(2n{+}2k{+}2{-}\rho)\,(2n{+}2k{+}1{-}\rho)}
       {(2n{+}2k{+}1)^2(2n{+}2k{+}2)^2\,(\rho{-}n{-}k)}\,,\label{eq:sigma}\\
  \tilde r(n,\rho)\;&:=\;\frac{-(2\rho)(2\rho{-}1)(2n{+}8{-}\rho)}{(\rho{-}n{-}3)(\rho{+}n{+}3)(\rho{+}1)^2}\,,\label{eq:rt}
\end{align}
so that by Lemma~\ref{lem:ratios} (iterated), wherever the terms are nonzero,
$\sigma_i=t(n{+}i,\rho)/t(n{+}4,\rho)$ and $\tilde r=t(n{+}4,\rho{+}1)/t(n{+}4,\rho)$
(in particular $\sigma_4=1$). Define the certificate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cert}
  \Rt(n,\rho)\;:=\;\frac{\Xh(n,\rho)}
  {4(n{+}3)(n{+}4)^2(2n{+}7)^2\,(\rho{-}n{-}1)(\rho{-}n{-}2)(\rho{-}n{-}3)}\,,
\end{equation}
where $\Xh\in\ZZ[n,\rho]$ is an explicit polynomial with $\deg_\rho\Xh=11$ and
$\deg_n\Xh=20$, divisible by $\rho^2$; its full coefficient list ($\sim$180 integers) is embedded
in the verification script (\texttt{code/verify\_a269021.py}, table \texttt{XHAT}) rather than
printed here. One anchor a reader can check by eye: the leading coefficient
$[\rho^{11}]\,\Xh$ equals $64n^{10}+1968n^9+26156n^8+198469n^7+952323n^6+3012795n^5+6333869n^4
+8663374n^3+7264534n^2+3266000n+549760$ --- precisely the degree-$10$ factor of $c_0(n)$.

\begin{proposition}[the two identities]\label{prop:ids}
As identities of rational functions,
\begin{align}
  \sum_{i=0}^{4}c_i(n)\,\sigma_i(n,\rho)
   \;&=\;\Rt(n,\rho{+}1)\,\tilde r(n,\rho)\;-\;\Rt(n,\rho)
   &&\text{in }\QQ(n,\rho), \tag{$\diamondsuit$}\label{eq:diamond}\\[2pt]
  \sum_{c=0}^{3}\sum_{i=0}^{4}c_i(n)\,\frac{t(n{+}i,n{+}c)}{t(n{+}4,n{+}4)}
   \;&=\;\Rt(n,n{+}4)
   &&\text{in }\QQ(n), \tag{$\clubsuit$}\label{eq:club}
\end{align}
where in \eqref{eq:club} each ratio $t(n{+}i,n{+}c)/t(n{+}4,n{+}4)$ denotes its closed rational
form in $n$ (fixed factorial offsets; spelled out in the script and guarded there against direct
factorial values).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each side of \eqref{eq:diamond} is a rational function; clearing the explicit product of all
denominators turns the difference into a polynomial $E(n,\rho)$. Summing the structural degrees
of the factors in \eqref{eq:sigma}--\eqref{eq:cert} (the bookkeeping is carried out
programmatically in the script, function \texttt{bound\_from\_terms}) bounds
$\deg_n E\le 89$ and $\deg_\rho E\le 28$. A nonzero polynomial with
$\deg_n\le B_N$, $\deg_\rho\le B_R$ cannot vanish on a grid of $(B_N{+}1)\times(B_R{+}1)$ points:
fixing any grid row $n=n_0$ gives a univariate polynomial in $\rho$ of degree $\le B_R$ vanishing
at $B_R{+}1$ points, hence identically; then each coefficient, a polynomial in $n$ of degree
$\le B_N$, vanishes at $B_N{+}1$ points. We evaluate $E$ exactly (rational arithmetic) at the
$90\times29$ grid $n\in\{500,\dots,589\}$, $\rho\in\{5000,\dots,5028\}$, whose points avoid every
zero of every denominator (all denominator factors are of the forms $\rho-n-k$, $2n+k-\rho$,
$\rho+n+k$, $\rho+1$, $n+k$, $2n+k$ with $|k|\le9$, none of which vanish there); all $2610$
values are $0$. Identity \eqref{eq:club} is proved the same way in one variable, with the
structural degree bound $B_J=351$ and $352$ evaluation points. Both computations are part of the
one-command verification (Section~\ref{sec:repro}) and complete in seconds.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This grid argument is a deterministic identity test, not a probabilistic one: the degree bounds
are explicit and the grid strictly exceeds them, so the vanishing forces the polynomial to be
zero. It replaces a symbolic normalization of rational functions whose expanded numerators have
hundreds of terms; nothing about it is numerical in the floating-point sense.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Telescoping}

\begin{lemma}[faithfulness at lattice points]\label{lem:faithful}
Let $n\ge1$ and $\rho\ge n+4$ be integers. Then:
(a) no denominator appearing in $\sigma_i$, $\tilde r$, $\Rt(n,\rho)$ or $\Rt(n,\rho{+}1)$
vanishes;
(b) $\sigma_i(n,\rho)\,t(n{+}4,\rho)=t(n{+}i,\rho)$ for all $0\le i\le4$;
(c) $\tilde r(n,\rho)\,t(n{+}4,\rho)=t(n{+}4,\rho{+}1)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) For $\rho\ge n+4$: the factors $\rho-n-k$ ($k\le3$) are $\ge1$; $\rho+n+k$, $\rho+1$,
$2n+2k+1$, $2n+2k+2$, $n+3$, $n+4$, $2n+7$ are positive; and in $\Rt(n,\rho{+}1)$ the shifted
factors $\rho-n$, $\rho-n-1$, $\rho-n-2$ are $\ge2$.
(b) On $n+4\le\rho\le 2n+2i$, where both terms are nonzero, this is Lemma~\ref{lem:ratios}
iterated. For $2n+2i<\rho\le2n+8$ (where $t(n{+}i,\rho)=0$ but $t(n{+}4,\rho)\ne0$): the
numerator of $\sigma_i$ contains the factor $\prod_{k=i}^{3}(2n{+}2k{+}2{-}\rho)(2n{+}2k{+}1{-}\rho)
=\prod_{s=2i+1}^{8}(2n{+}s{-}\rho)$, which vanishes at exactly those integer $\rho$ --- so both
sides are $0$, as \emph{polynomial} vanishing, with no limit taken. For $\rho>2n+8$ both terms
vanish and $\sigma_i$ is finite by (a).
(c) Same argument: for $n+4\le\rho<2n+8$ it is Lemma~\ref{lem:ratios}; at $\rho=2n+8$ the factor
$(2n{+}8{-}\rho)$ makes the left side $0=t(n{+}4,2n{+}9)$; for $\rho>2n+8$ both sides vanish.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:telescope}
For every integer $n\ge5$, $\;\sum_{\rho\in\ZZ}(\Lop t)(n,\rho)=0$; that is,
$(\Lop a)(n)=0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define $G^{*}(n,\rho)$ for integer $\rho$ by
\[
  G^{*}(n,\rho)\;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    0, & \rho\le n,\\[1pt]
    \displaystyle\sum_{q=n}^{\rho-1}(\Lop t)(n,q), & n+1\le\rho\le n+3,\\[6pt]
    \Rt(n,\rho)\,t(n{+}4,\rho), & \rho\ge n+4.
  \end{cases}
\]
We claim $(\Lop t)(n,\rho)=G^{*}(n,\rho{+}1)-G^{*}(n,\rho)$ for \emph{every} integer $\rho$.
For $\rho\le n-1$ both sides are $0$ ($t(n{+}i,\cdot)$ is supported on $[n{+}i,2n{+}2i]$, and
$n\ge5$ keeps all cumulative indices in range). For $\rho\in\{n,n{+}1,n{+}2\}$ it is the
definition of the cumulative branch. For $\rho=n+3$ it reads
$\sum_{q=n}^{n+3}(\Lop t)(n,q)=\Rt(n,n{+}4)\,t(n{+}4,n{+}4)$, which is \eqref{eq:club}
multiplied by $t(n{+}4,n{+}4)\neq0$; here each closed form in \eqref{eq:club} equals the true
ratio $t(n{+}i,n{+}c)/t(n{+}4,n{+}4)$ for every integer $n\ge5$ by the same fixed-offset
factorial manipulations as Lemma~\ref{lem:ratios} (all factorial arguments are nonnegative for
$n\ge5$, and the vanishing cases $c<i$ are encoded by the empty binomial; the script additionally
spot-checks all twenty forms against direct factorial values). For $\rho\ge n+4$, multiply
\eqref{eq:diamond} by $t(n{+}4,\rho)$ and apply Lemma~\ref{lem:faithful}:
the left side becomes $(\Lop t)(n,\rho)$ and the right side
$\Rt(n,\rho{+}1)t(n{+}4,\rho{+}1)-\Rt(n,\rho)t(n{+}4,\rho)=G^{*}(n,\rho{+}1)-G^{*}(n,\rho)$.
Finally $G^{*}(n,\rho)=0$ for $\rho>2n+8$ (there $t(n{+}4,\rho)=0$ while $\Rt$ is finite by
Lemma~\ref{lem:faithful}(a), since $\rho>2n+8>n+3$ keeps its denominator nonzero). Summing the
telescoped identity over the finite support: $\sum_{\rho\in\ZZ}(\Lop t)(n,\rho)=
\lim_{\rho\to\infty}G^{*}-\lim_{\rho\to-\infty}G^{*}=0-0$. By \eqref{eq:star},
$\sum_{\rho}(\Lop t)(n,\rho)=\sum_i c_i(n)a(n{+}i)=(\Lop a)(n)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{main}]
Theorem~\ref{thm:telescope} gives $(\Lop a)(n)=0$ for $n\ge5$. For $0\le n\le4$ the relation is
a finite integer identity, verified directly on the values computed from \eqref{eq:star} (the
script checks $n=0,\dots,52$; the research record checked $n\le80$ against an independent
Toeplitz-determinant engine). This proves \eqref{eq:Lrec} for all $n\ge0$. For the equivalence
with the $\tilde a$-form of \cite{KK23}: substituting $\tilde a_{n+i}=a(n{+}i)/(2n{+}2i)!^{2}$
into their Conjecture~17 and multiplying by $(2n{+}8)!^{2}$ yields exactly
$4(n{+}3)(n{+}4)^2(2n{+}7)^2$ times \eqref{eq:Lrec} (a polynomial-coefficient identity checked in
the script at points beyond its degree bound); the factor is positive for $n\ge0$, so the two
statements are equivalent.
\end{proof}

\section{Validation and reproducibility}\label{sec:repro}

Everything is re-checkable with one command and no external data:
\begin{lstlisting}
python code/verify_a269021.py     # expect "A269021 VERIFICATION: ALL 12 CHECKS PASS"
\end{lstlisting}
The script is Python~3 standard library only ($\sim$10\,s). Its twelve checks, in order:
brute force over \emph{all} $(2n)!$ permutations for $n\le3$ against \eqref{eq:star}; the
hook-length sum \eqref{eq:rsk} for $n\le10$; the $16$ OEIS-listed terms; $\Lop$ annihilating the
representation values for $n\le52$; the ratio closed forms of Lemma~\ref{lem:ratios} against
direct factorial ratios; identity \eqref{eq:diamond} on its $90\times29$ grid; the junction
ratios against factorial values; identity \eqref{eq:club} on its $352$-point grid; exhaustive
telescoping ($(\Lop t)(n,\rho)=\Delta_\rho G^{*}$ at \emph{every} integer $\rho$, and
$\sum_\rho(\Lop t)=0$, for $5\le n\le40$); and the equivalence with the $\tilde a$-form of
\cite{KK23}. The conjectured coefficients $c_i$ and the certificate numerator $\Xh$ are embedded
in the script verbatim; the empirical OEIS terms are used \emph{only} as anchors, never as proof
input.

During the research phase the same material passed a larger battery: \eqref{eq:star} against $81$
independently computed terms, \eqref{eq:starprime} against the exact LIS-distribution array for
$n\le26$, the grid identities at independently reconstructed certificates, and an independent
automated audit that re-ran all computations and re-derived the small cases from separately
written code. This paper's source and the verification script are publicly available at
\url{https://github.com/alejlizardi/Periapsis_papers} (directory

\section{Remarks and further work}

\begin{remark}[D-finiteness]
As of \cite{KK23}, even the \emph{existence} of some linear recurrence for $a(n)$ (equivalently
for $\tilde a_n$) was open --- they classify the sequence in their weakest tier, with neither
the guessed recurrence nor D-finiteness proved, and we are not aware of a proof having appeared
since. Representation \eqref{eq:star} settles existence a priori: a finite sum of a proper
hypergeometric term is P-recursive by the general Wilf--Zeilberger theory~\cite{WZ92},
independently of the particular certificate exhibited here.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[the coupled-diagonal regime]
The obstruction named in \cite{KK23} --- fixed-pattern D-finiteness says nothing once the pattern
length is coupled to the permutation length --- is genuine in general: the two-variable array
$u_k(m)=\#\{\sigma\in S_m:\LIS(\sigma)\le k\}$ is provably not bivariate-holonomic (the order of
its minimal $m$-recurrence grows with $k$). What makes the diagonal $k\approx m/2$ accessible is
Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc}: in the upper-half regime the determinantal gap expansion collapses to its
one-point term, producing a \emph{single} hypergeometric sum. The same collapse gives
\eqref{eq:starprime} for the whole upper half of the LIS distribution, and two-row/two-point
truncations of the same kind may make neighboring diagonals (e.g.\ $\LIS\ge n-1$ on $S_{2n}$,
where at most two rows can be long) similarly finite --- we have not pursued this.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[the operator's other solutions]
Kauers and Koutschan observe that the recurrence has the hypergeometric term $(n-1)(2n)!$ among
its solutions. This is consistent with, and independent of, Theorem~\ref{main}: the order-$4$
operator $\Lop$ has a four-dimensional solution space, containing both that hypergeometric
solution and $a(n)$ itself. The two are genuinely different solutions: by Kotesovec's asymptotic
recorded on the OEIS entry, $a(n)\sim 16^{\,n}(n-1)!/(\pi e^{2})$, so $a(n)/(2n)!\to0$
super-exponentially --- permutations of $[2n]$ with an increasing $n$-subsequence are a vanishing
fraction of $S_{2n}$, even though their count satisfies this small recurrence.
\end{remark}
