\begin{abstract}
For a permutation $\sigma$ of $\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$, let $H_\sigma$ be the two-edge-colored
multigraph on $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$ whose blue edges form the natural-order Hamiltonian path
and whose red edges form the Hamiltonian path induced by the value order of $\sigma$,
and let $\rho(\sigma)$ denote the maximum number of vertices on a properly colored path
of $H_\sigma$ (a simple path whose consecutive edges alternate in color). Every
two-edge-colored multigraph that is the union of two Hamiltonian paths on a common
vertex set arises this way. We prove that
$\pmin(n) := \min_{\sigma} \rho(\sigma) = O(\sqrt{n}\,)$; explicitly,
$\pmin(n) \le 8\sqrt{n} + 20$ for all $n \ge 100$. Consequently
$\pmin(n)/n \to 0$: there is no constant $c>0$ such that the union of two Hamiltonian
paths must contain a properly colored path on $cn$ vertices. This answers, in the
negative, the main question posed in the author's earlier note \cite{Liz26}, where a
positive answer was proposed as a route toward improved bounds for Lov\'asz's problem
on Hamiltonian paths in vertex-transitive graphs; we emphasize that the present result
closes only that particular route, not the underlying matching-edge question
(Section~\ref{subsec:scope}).

The proof introduces an inflation (substitution) operation on permutations together
with a per-permutation invariant, the \emph{transit number} $M(\pi)$, which bounds how
many vertices a properly colored path can collect while passing through an inflated
copy of $\pi$. Our main lemma is a complete classification of the constrained
terminal-to-terminal paths (\emph{admissible visits}) of an explicit gadget family:
for every even $b \ge 6$, the permutation
$T_b = (0,\, b{-}1,\, 2,\, 3,\, \dots,\, b{-}2,\, 1)$ admits exactly five admissible
visits, of sizes $1, 2, 2, 3, 3$; in particular $M(T_b) = 3$, independent of $b$. The
classification turns on a parity obstruction in a long run of doubled edges.
For the resulting explicit permutations $\sigma_{a,b}$ on $n = ab$ vertices we prove
the two-sided bounds $2a + 2b - 7 \le \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \le 4a + 2b$, so the family
provably realizes the order $\sqrt n$ when $a \asymp b$. Whether $\pmin(n) = o(n)$ can
be complemented by any superconstant lower bound remains open: even
$\pmin(n) \to \infty$ is not known. A second, independent construction with the same
conclusion is given in an appendix, and all computations are reproducible from the
ancillary code and logs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Let $S_n$ denote the set of bijections
$\sigma\colon \{0,1,\dots,n-1\}\to\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$.
Following \cite{Liz26}, associate with $\sigma \in S_n$ the two-edge-colored multigraph
$H_\sigma$ on vertex set $\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}$ with
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{P-edges} (``position'', blue): $\{i, i+1\}$ for $0 \le i \le n-2$;
\item \textbf{V-edges} (``value'', red): $\{\inv\sigma(v), \inv\sigma(v+1)\}$ for
$0 \le v \le n-2$.
\end{itemize}
Each color class is a Hamiltonian path. A vertex pair may carry both a P-edge and a
V-edge; these are retained as \emph{parallel edges of different colors} (``doubled
pairs''). Conversely, the union of any two Hamiltonian paths on a common vertex set,
with one path blue and the other red, is color-isomorphic to $H_\sigma$ for some
$\sigma$ (identify the vertex set with $\{0,\dots,n-1\}$ along the blue path, and let
$\sigma$ rank the vertices along the red path).

A \emph{properly colored path} (PC path) is a simple path---all vertices
distinct---whose consecutive edges have different colors. A single vertex is a PC
path. Let $\rho(\sigma)$ be the maximum number of vertices on a PC path in $H_\sigma$,
and
\[
\pmin(n) \;=\; \min_{\sigma\in S_n} \rho(\sigma) .
\]

The note \cite{Liz26}, an earlier study by the present author, developed a local
calculus for PC paths in $H_\sigma$, determined the extremal value of $\rho$ on a
family of block-reversal permutations, refuted a half-bound conjecture at $n = 18$,
and exhibited stacked families with $\rho/n$ tending (conjecturally) to $5/11$ and
then $10/28$. The main question it left open, stated there in self-contained form,
was:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Does there exist $c>0$ such that every two-edge-colored multigraph that is the
union of two Hamiltonian paths on the same vertex set contains a properly colored
simple path on at least $cn$ vertices?}
\end{quote}

The original motivation comes from a relaxation chain described in \cite{Liz26}: a
linear lower bound on $\pmin(n)$ would transfer, through an exact conversion lemma, to
a linear lower bound on a matching-edge parameter $f(n)$ for a family of subcubic
graphs arising in the Norin--Steiner--Thomass\'e--Wollan approach \cite{NSTW25} to
Lov\'asz's 1969 question \cite{Lov70} on Hamiltonian paths in connected
vertex-transitive graphs. We stress the scope of that motivation: the relaxation was
proposed in \cite{Liz26}, not in the broader literature, and the present paper's
negative answer closes only this relaxation (see Section~\ref{subsec:scope}).

Beyond that specific motivation, the question is a natural extremal problem about
alternating paths. Properly colored (``alternating'') paths and cycles in edge-colored
graphs have been studied extensively since the survey of Bang-Jensen and Gutin
\cite{BJG97}; see also \cite{GK09,ABMMM10}. The positive results in that line are
sufficient conditions---typically large color degree or dense host graphs---for long
PC paths or cycles to exist. The graphs $H_\sigma$ sit at the opposite end of the
spectrum: every vertex has at most two edges of each color, and the question is
extremal rather than Dirac-type. We are not aware of prior work on the union of two
Hamiltonian paths besides \cite{Liz26}.

\subsection{Results}

We answer the question in the negative.

\begin{theorem}[Main Theorem]\label{thm:main-intro}
$\pmin(n) = O(\sqrt n\,)$. Explicitly, $\pmin(n) \le 8\sqrt n + 20$ for all
$n \ge 100$. Consequently $\pmin(n)/n \to 0$.
\end{theorem}

The construction is explicit. For even $b\ge 6$ let
\[
T_b \;=\; (0,\; b{-}1,\; 2,\; 3,\; \dots,\; b{-}2,\; 1) \in S_b
\]
be the identity permutation with the values $1$ and $b-1$ exchanged, and for $a \ge 2$
define $\sigma_{a,b} \in S_{ab}$ by
\[
\sigma_{a,b}(jb + r) \;=\; jb + T_b(r) \qquad (0 \le j < a,\ 0 \le r < b),
\]
i.e.\ $a$ consecutive copies of $T_b$ on consecutive value ranges. We prove the
two-sided estimate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:twosided}
2a + 2b - 7 \;\le\; \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \;\le\; 4a + 2b
\qquad (a \ge 2,\ b \ge 6 \text{ even}),
\end{equation}
the upper bound by the inflation calculus below
(Theorem~\ref{thm:main}, Proposition~\ref{prop:refined}), the lower bound by an
explicit path (Proposition~\ref{prop:lower}). Thus $\rho(\sigma_{a,b}) = \Theta(a+b)$:
with $a \asymp b \asymp \sqrt n$ the family realizes the order $\sqrt n$ exactly, so
the upper bound is tight for this family and not an artifact of a construction whose
true value is smaller. (For $a = 2$ both bounds in \eqref{eq:twosided} are weak: indeed
$\rho(\sigma_{2,b}) \le n = 2b$ trivially. The construction is informative for
$a \ge 3$, and asymptotically for $a \to \infty$.) Exact machine computation,
reproducible from the ancillary files, gives $\rho(\sigma_{a,b}) = 2a + 2b - 4$ at
every tested point up to $n = 720$ (Section~\ref{sec:comp}); we use this exact
formula nowhere in the proofs and state it only as data.

The proof of the upper bounds has two independent components.

\paragraph{1. An inflation calculus (Sections~\ref{sec:ports}--\ref{sec:inflation}).}
Call a set $B$ of consecutive positions whose $\sigma$-image is a set of consecutive
values an \emph{interval block}. A short argument (Lemma~\ref{lem:ports}) shows that
at most \emph{four} edges of $H_\sigma$ join $B$ to its complement, and that they
attach at four canonical \emph{terminal} vertices of $B$, with prescribed colors. If
$\sigma = \tau[\pi_1,\dots,\pi_a]$ is built by inflation (each position block an
interval block with pattern $\pi_i$, arranged by an outer permutation $\tau$), then
any PC path decomposes into at most $2a-1$ \emph{visits} to blocks, and each visit
other than the first and last must enter and leave through terminals subject to color
constraints. Defining the \emph{transit number} $M(\pi)$ as the largest number of
vertices such a constrained passage (\emph{admissible visit}) through $H_\pi$ can
collect, we obtain (Theorem~\ref{thm:inflation})
\[
\rho\bigl(\tau[\pi_1,\dots,\pi_a]\bigr) \;\le\; (2a-3)\,\max_i M(\pi_i) \;+\;
2\,\max_i |\pi_i| .
\]

\paragraph{2. A classification of the gadget's admissible visits
(Section~\ref{sec:gadget}).}
For every even $b \ge 6$ we determine the admissible visits of $H_{T_b}$
\emph{completely} (Theorem~\ref{thm:classification}): up to reversal there are
exactly five, of sizes $1, 2, 2, 3, 3$. In particular
\[
M(T_b) \;=\; 3 ,
\]
independent of $b$. The mechanism is a parity obstruction: $H_{T_b}$ contains a long
run of doubled edges which any terminal-to-terminal passage must either avoid or
cross completely, and a full crossing forces the color of the departing edge
precisely so that, for even $b$, every continuation is blocked. For odd $b$ the
parity reverses and a transit can collect all $b$ vertices; exact computation
confirms $M(T_b) = b$ for odd $b$. The even/odd contrast shows that the parity, not
some feature of the argument, is what bounds the transit number.

Combining the two components and balancing $a$ against $b$ yields
Theorem~\ref{thm:main-intro}; the crude bound $\rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \le 6a + 2b$,
which needs only the value $M(T_b) = 3$ and not the full classification, already
gives $\pmin(n) \le 10\sqrt n + 10$, and the refined constant $8\sqrt n + 20$ uses
the classification once more (Proposition~\ref{prop:refined}). The proofs are
elementary and self-contained; the computations reported in Section~\ref{sec:comp}
are independent verifications, not ingredients of the proofs.

Appendix~\ref{app:trap} contains a \emph{second, independent} construction proving
$\pmin(n) = O(\sqrt n)$: chains of reversed odd blocks separated by singletons, where
a different parity trap blocks every crossing, with its own complete proof. The two
constructions share the parity-trap idea but are different families with different
edge inventories, and they were found and verified independently, by different
methods and different solvers. We give both because a redundant proof of the main
claim seems worth the space.

Section~\ref{sec:disc} isolates what is known about lower bounds, including the
machine-verified refutation of a natural conjectured inequality
($\rho \ge$ number of $\pm1$-blocks) that holds exhaustively through $n = 12$ but
fails at $n = 240$.

\subsection{Scope: what this does and does not say about Lov\'asz's
problem}\label{subsec:scope}

The conversion lemma of \cite{Liz26} maps PC paths in $H_\sigma$ to
matching-edge-rich paths in an associated subcubic graph $G(n,\sigma)$
\emph{one-directionally}. Our construction makes properly colored paths short; it
says nothing about paths in $G(n,\sigma)$ that are allowed to use runs of
same-colored edges. The known bound $f(n) = \Omega(n^{4/5})$ for the matching-edge
parameter, which rests on the circumference theorem for $3$-connected cubic graphs of
Liu, Yu and Zhang \cite{LYZ18} as used in \cite{NSTW25}, is untouched, and
$f(n) = \Theta(n)$ remains entirely possible. What dies is only the hope, raised in
\cite{Liz26}, of proving $f(n) = \Omega(n)$ via the properly-colored relaxation.

On the lower-bound side our knowledge is thin: we prove no superconstant lower bound
on $\pmin(n)$, and to our knowledge even $\pmin(n) \to \infty$ is open. The
quantities controlling the natural approaches are discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:disc}, where we also state the conjecture
$\pmin(n) = \Theta(\sqrt n)$ --- at present an upper bound plus structural
circumstantial evidence, no more.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}

Throughout, $\sigma \in S_n$ and $H_\sigma$ are as in Section~\ref{sec:intro}; colors
are $\mathrm P$ and $\mathrm V$, and for a color $c$ we write $\bar c$ for the other
color. Paths are sequences of distinct vertices $u_1,\dots,u_k$ ($k \ge 1$) together
with, for each consecutive pair, a chosen edge (with its color) joining them; in a
doubled pair the path commits to one of the two parallel edges. A path is
\emph{properly colored} (PC) if consecutive edges have different colors. The
\emph{size} of a path is its number of vertices $k$. Since each vertex pair carries
at most one edge of each color and paths have distinct vertices, a path uses pairwise
distinct edges, even in the multigraph sense.

\begin{observation}\label{obs:translate}
A two-edge-colored multigraph is the union of a blue and a red Hamiltonian path on a
common vertex set of size $n$ if and only if it is color-isomorphic to $H_\sigma$ for
some $\sigma \in S_n$. Hence Theorem~\ref{thm:main-intro} is equivalent to its
abstract formulation.
\end{observation}

\section{Interval blocks and ports}\label{sec:ports}

\begin{definition}[Interval block, pattern]\label{def:block}
An \emph{interval block} of $\sigma \in S_n$ is a set of positions
$B = \{p_\ell, p_\ell + 1, \dots, p_r\}$ whose image is a set of consecutive values
$\sigma(B) = \{w_\ell, \dots, w_r\}$. Its \emph{pattern} is the permutation
$\pi \in S_b$, $b = |B|$, defined by $\pi(t) = \sigma(p_\ell + t) - w_\ell$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Port structure]\label{lem:ports}
Let $B$ be an interval block of $\sigma$ with positions $[p_\ell,p_r]$, values
$[w_\ell,w_r]$, and pattern $\pi$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] The sub-multigraph of $H_\sigma$ induced on $B$ is color-isomorphic to
$H_\pi$ under the map $p \mapsto p - p_\ell$.
\item[(b)] Every edge of $H_\sigma$ with exactly one endpoint in $B$ is one of at
most four: the P-edges $\{p_\ell - 1, p_\ell\}$ and $\{p_r, p_r + 1\}$ (when they
exist), whose endpoints in $B$ are $p_\ell$ and $p_r$; and the V-edges
$\{\inv\sigma(w_\ell - 1), \inv\sigma(w_\ell)\}$ and
$\{\inv\sigma(w_r), \inv\sigma(w_r + 1)\}$ (when they exist), whose endpoints in $B$
are $\inv\sigma(w_\ell)$ and $\inv\sigma(w_r)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) P-edges internal to $B$ are the pairs $\{p, p+1\} \subseteq [p_\ell, p_r]$, which
correspond exactly to the P-edges of $H_\pi$. A V-edge
$\{\inv\sigma(v), \inv\sigma(v+1)\}$ has both endpoints in $B$ if and only if both
$v, v+1 \in [w_\ell, w_r]$ (as $\sigma(B) = [w_\ell, w_r]$ and $\sigma$ is a
bijection), and these edges correspond exactly to the V-edges of $H_\pi$ under the
stated map, since $\inv\pi(v - w_\ell) = \inv\sigma(v) - p_\ell$ for
$v \in [w_\ell, w_r]$. Doubled pairs match doubled pairs, so the isomorphism
preserves the multigraph structure with colors.

(b) A P-edge $\{p, p+1\}$ with exactly one endpoint in the interval $[p_\ell, p_r]$
satisfies $p + 1 = p_\ell$ or $p = p_r$. A V-edge
$\{\inv\sigma(v), \inv\sigma(v+1)\}$ with exactly one endpoint in $B$ has exactly one
of $v, v+1$ in $[w_\ell, w_r]$, forcing $v + 1 = w_\ell$ or $v = w_r$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Terminals, roles, port colors]\label{def:terminals}
Let $\pi \in S_b$, $b \ge 2$. The \emph{terminals} of $H_\pi$ are the four
\emph{roles}
\[
p_0 = \text{vertex } 0, \quad
p_1 = \text{vertex } b-1, \quad
v_0 = \text{vertex } \inv\pi(0), \quad
v_1 = \text{vertex } \inv\pi(b-1),
\]
with \emph{port colors} $\pc(p_0) = \pc(p_1) = \mathrm P$ and
$\pc(v_0) = \pc(v_1) = \mathrm V$. Distinct roles may occupy the same vertex (a role
is a label, not a vertex); since $b \ge 2$, two roles with the \emph{same} port color
always occupy distinct vertices.
\end{definition}

By Lemma~\ref{lem:ports}, in any interval block with pattern $\pi$ the at most four
outgoing edges attach (inside the block) precisely at the terminals, and the color of
an outgoing edge equals the port color of the role at which it attaches.

\begin{definition}[Admissible visit, transit number]\label{def:M}
Let $\pi \in S_b$, $b \ge 2$. An \emph{admissible visit} of $H_\pi$ is either
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] a PC path $u_1, \dots, u_k$ in $H_\pi$ with $k \ge 2$, together with a
choice of roles $r$ at $u_1$ and $r'$ at $u_k$, such that the first edge has color
$\ne \pc(r)$ and the last edge has color $\ne \pc(r')$; or
\item[(ii)] a single vertex carrying two distinct roles $r, r'$ with
$\pc(r) \ne \pc(r')$.
\end{enumerate}
The \emph{transit number} $M(\pi)$ is the maximum size of an admissible visit, and
$M(\pi) = 0$ if none exists. For $b \le 1$ set $M(\pi) = 1$.
\end{definition}

Since a classification of admissible visits must account for the role choices, we
record two trivial but load-bearing facts.

\begin{lemma}[Reversal; endpoint constraint]\label{lem:reversal}
(a) If $(u_1, \dots, u_k; r, r')$ is an admissible visit, so is the reversed
$(u_k, \dots, u_1; r', r)$. Hence admissible visits may be classified as undirected
vertex sequences with unordered role pairs.
(b) In a type-(i) admissible visit, both $u_1$ and $u_k$ are terminal vertices, the
roles $r, r'$ are distinct, and with two colors the conditions read: first edge color
$= \overline{\pc(r)}$, last edge color $= \overline{\pc(r')}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) Reversal swaps first and last edges and the two endpoint constraints. (b) The
roles occupy $u_1 \ne u_k$, hence are distinct; the rest is the definition.
\end{proof}

\section{Inflation and the Inflation Lemma}\label{sec:inflation}

\begin{definition}[Inflation]\label{def:inflation}
Let $a \ge 2$, let $\tau$ be a permutation of $\{1, \dots, a\}$, and let
$\pi_1, \dots, \pi_a$ be permutations of sizes $b_1, \dots, b_a \ge 2$,
$N = \sum_i b_i$. Put $s_i = \sum_{j < i} b_j$ (position offsets) and
$t_i = \sum_{j:\ \tau(j) < \tau(i)} b_j$ (value offsets). The
\emph{inflation} $\sigma = \tau[\pi_1, \dots, \pi_a] \in S_N$ is defined by
\[
\sigma(s_i + r) = t_i + \pi_i(r), \qquad i = 1, \dots, a,\quad 0 \le r < b_i .
\]
\end{definition}

The value intervals $[t_i, t_i + b_i - 1]$ partition $\{0, \dots, N-1\}$, so $\sigma$
is a bijection. Each position block $B_i = [s_i, s_i + b_i - 1]$ is an interval block
of $\sigma$ with value interval $[t_i, t_i + b_i - 1]$ and pattern $\pi_i$, so
Lemma~\ref{lem:ports} applies to all blocks simultaneously. We call edges of
$H_\sigma$ with endpoints in different blocks \emph{cross edges}.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:crosscount}
$H_{\tau[\pi_1,\dots,\pi_a]}$ has exactly $2(a-1)$ cross edges, counted with
multiplicity: $a-1$ P-colored (one per consecutive pair of position blocks) and
$a-1$ V-colored (one per consecutive pair of value intervals). Each block is incident
to at most $4$ cross edges, attaching at its terminals as in
Lemma~\ref{lem:ports}(b), and each terminal role of each block carries at most one
cross edge.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
A P-edge $\{p, p+1\}$ is a cross edge iff $p+1 \in \{s_2, \dots, s_a\}$: $a-1$ edges.
A V-edge $\{\inv\sigma(v), \inv\sigma(v+1)\}$ is a cross edge iff $v, v+1$ lie in
different value intervals, which happens for exactly the $a-1$ values $v$ ending a
value interval. The attachment statement is Lemma~\ref{lem:ports}(b); per block, each
of the four port positions (P at each end of the position interval, V at each end of
the value interval) is met by at most one cross edge.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Visit decomposition]\label{lem:visits}
Let $\sigma = \tau[\pi_1, \dots, \pi_a]$, $a \ge 2$, and let $Q$ be a PC path in
$H_\sigma$. Partition the vertex sequence of $Q$ into maximal runs of consecutive
vertices lying in a common block; call these runs \emph{visits}. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] Consecutive visits are joined by exactly one cross edge; distinct
consecutive pairs use distinct cross edges. Hence if $Q$ has $q$ visits, then
$q - 1 \le 2(a-1)$, i.e.\ $q \le 2a - 1$.
\item[(b)] Every visit that is neither the first nor the last is an admissible visit
of the copy of $H_{\pi_i}$ on its block; in particular it has at most $M(\pi_i)$
vertices.
\item[(c)] The first and the last visit have at most $\max_i b_i$ vertices each.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) The edge of $Q$ joining the last vertex of one visit to the first vertex of the
next has endpoints in different blocks (by maximality of runs), so it is a cross
edge. $Q$ has distinct vertices, hence pairwise distinct edges, so the $q-1$
connecting edges are distinct cross edges; by Lemma~\ref{lem:crosscount} there are
$2(a-1)$ of them in all.

(b) Let $W = u_1, \dots, u_k$ be an interior visit in block $B_i$. There is an
\emph{entry} cross edge $e_{\mathrm{in}}$ of $Q$ arriving at $u_1$ and an \emph{exit}
cross edge $e_{\mathrm{out}}$ leaving $u_k$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:crosscount},
$e_{\mathrm{in}}$ attaches at a terminal role $r$ at $u_1$ and has color $\pc(r)$;
likewise $e_{\mathrm{out}}$ at a role $r'$ at $u_k$ with color $\pc(r')$. All edges
of $Q$ between vertices of $W$ have both endpoints in $B_i$, so under
Lemma~\ref{lem:ports}(a) the run $W$ is a PC path in the copy of $H_{\pi_i}$. If
$k \ge 2$: the PC condition of $Q$ at $u_1$ (between $e_{\mathrm{in}}$ and the first
edge of $W$) forces the first edge of $W$ to have color $\ne \pc(r)$, and similarly
at $u_k$; thus $W$, with the roles $r, r'$, is admissible of type (i). If $k = 1$:
the PC condition at $u_1$ between $e_{\mathrm{in}}$ and $e_{\mathrm{out}}$ forces
$\pc(r) \ne \pc(r')$, so $u_1$ carries two roles of different port colors:
admissible of type (ii).

(c) A visit lies in one block, so its size is at most that block's size.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Inflation Lemma]\label{thm:inflation}
For $a \ge 2$,
\[
\rho\bigl(\tau[\pi_1, \dots, \pi_a]\bigr)
\;\le\;
(2a - 3)\,\max_i M(\pi_i) \;+\; 2\,\max_i b_i .
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $Q$ attain $\rho$ and let $q$ be its number of visits. If $q = 1$, then
$\rho \le \max_i b_i$ and the bound holds. If $q \ge 2$, then by
Lemma~\ref{lem:visits} the $q - 2 \le 2a - 3$ interior visits have at most
$\max_i M(\pi_i)$ vertices each and the two end visits at most $\max_i b_i$ each;
visits partition the vertices of $Q$.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:inflation} holds for \emph{every} outer permutation $\tau$; no
property of $\tau$ is used. The problem is thereby reduced to finding patterns with
small transit number relative to their size.

\section{The gadget: classification of admissible visits of
$H_{T_b}$}\label{sec:gadget}

\begin{definition}\label{def:Tb}
For even $b \ge 6$ let $T_b \in S_b$ be given by
\[
T_b(0) = 0, \qquad T_b(1) = b-1, \qquad T_b(i) = i \ \ (2 \le i \le b-2), \qquad
T_b(b-1) = 1 .
\]
\end{definition}

Thus $\inv{T_b}$ fixes $0$ and each $v \in [2, b-2]$, and maps $1 \mapsto b-1$,
$b-1 \mapsto 1$ ($T_b$ is an involution). We first record the complete edge
inventory; recall $b \ge 6$.

\begin{lemma}[Inventory]\label{lem:inventory}
The edges of $H_{T_b}$ are exactly:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{doubled pairs} $\{i, i+1\}$ for $2 \le i \le b-3$ (each carrying one P-
and one V-edge);
\item \emph{P-only edges} $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,2\}$, $\{b-2, b-1\}$;
\item \emph{V-only edges} $\{0, b-1\}$, $\{2, b-1\}$, $\{1, b-2\}$.
\end{itemize}
Consequently the full edge lists at the relevant vertices are:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{vertex } 0: & \mathrm P\,\{0,1\};\ \ \mathrm V\,\{0,b{-}1\} \quad
(\textit{degree } 2),\\[2pt]
\text{vertex } 1: & \mathrm P\,\{0,1\},\ \mathrm P\,\{1,2\};\ \ \mathrm V\,\{1,b{-}2\}
\quad (\textit{unique V-edge}),\\[2pt]
\text{vertex } b{-}1: & \mathrm P\,\{b{-}2,b{-}1\};\ \ \mathrm V\,\{0,b{-}1\},\
\mathrm V\,\{2,b{-}1\} \quad (\textit{unique P-edge}),\\[2pt]
\text{vertex } 2: & \mathrm P\,\{1,2\};\ \text{doubled } \{2,3\};\
\mathrm V\,\{2,b{-}1\},\\[2pt]
\text{vertex } b{-}2: & \text{doubled } \{b{-}3,b{-}2\};\ \mathrm P\,\{b{-}2,b{-}1\};\
\mathrm V\,\{1,b{-}2\},\\[2pt]
\text{vertex } i,\ 3 \le i \le b-3: & \text{doubled } \{i{-}1,i\} \text{ and }
\{i,i{+}1\} \text{ only}.
\end{array}
\]
The terminals are $p_0 = v_0 = \textit{vertex } 0$, $v_1 = \textit{vertex } 1$,
$p_1 = \textit{vertex } b-1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
P-edges are $\{i, i+1\}$, $0 \le i \le b-2$, by definition. The V-edges, indexed by
$v = 0, \dots, b-2$, are $\{\inv{T_b}(v), \inv{T_b}(v+1)\}$:
\[
v = 0:\ \{0, b{-}1\}; \qquad
v = 1:\ \{b{-}1, 2\}; \qquad
2 \le v \le b-3:\ \{v, v{+}1\}; \qquad
v = b{-}2:\ \{b{-}2, 1\}.
\]
A pair $\{i, i+1\}$ is doubled iff it occurs in both lists: this happens exactly for
$2 \le i \le b-3$. (For $b \ge 6$: $\{0,1\}$ would require
$|T_b(1) - T_b(0)| = b-1 = 1$, false; $\{1,2\}$ requires $b - 3 = 1$, false;
$\{b-2,b-1\}$ requires $b-3 = 1$, false. The three long V-edges join non-adjacent
positions since $b - 1, b - 3 \ge 3$.) The per-vertex lists and the terminals
($\inv{T_b}(0) = 0$, $\inv{T_b}(b-1) = 1$) follow by inspection.
\end{proof}

Let $S = \{2, 3, \dots, b-2\}$ (the \emph{segment}), so $|S| = b - 3$. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory}, the sub-multigraph induced on $S$ is the path
$2 - 3 - \cdots - (b-2)$ with every pair doubled; the only edges from $S$ to its
complement are the two at vertex $2$ (P $\{1,2\}$, V $\{2,b-1\}$) and the two at
vertex $b-2$ (P $\{b-2,b-1\}$, V $\{1,b-2\}$); and no terminal lies in $S$.

\begin{lemma}[Segment behavior]\label{lem:segment}
Let $Q$ be a PC path in $H_{T_b}$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] While inside $S$, $Q$ moves monotonically in position (each interior
vertex of $S$ is adjacent only to its two position-neighbors), and $Q$ can leave $S$
only from vertex $2$ or vertex $b-2$.
\item[(b)] Suppose $Q$ enters $S$ at one end ($2$ or $b-2$) by an edge of color $c$,
at a moment when no vertex of $S$ has yet been visited, and later leaves $S$. Then
either it leaves immediately from the entry vertex (using the other external edge
there, which has color $\bar c$), or it traverses all of $S$ to the far end; in the
latter case its $b-4$ internal edges alternate colors starting with $\bar c$, so
(since $b$ is even, making $b - 4$ even) the last internal edge has color $c$, and
any edge by which $Q$ then leaves $S$ has color $\bar c$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) is immediate from the inventory: a simple path cannot reverse inside a path
graph. For (b): leaving from the entry vertex means the run in $S$ is that single
vertex; the two external edges at $2$ (P $\{1,2\}$, V $\{2,b-1\}$) have different
colors, likewise at $b-2$, and the PC condition forces the exit edge to differ from
the entry edge, i.e.\ to be the other one, of color $\bar c$. Otherwise the run
proceeds monotonically and can only exit at the far end, having covered all of $S$
with internal edges $g_1, \dots, g_{b-4}$. The PC condition at the entry vertex gives
$g_1 = \bar c$ (both colors are available on every doubled pair; the PC condition is
what forces the alternation), alternation gives $g_m = \bar c$ for odd $m$ and
$g_m = c$ for even $m$, and $b - 4$ is even, so $g_{b-4} = c$; the PC condition at
the far end forces the exit edge to have color $\ne c$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The classification theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Classification of admissible visits]\label{thm:classification}
Let $b \ge 6$ be even. Up to reversal (Lemma~\ref{lem:reversal}), the admissible
visits of $H_{T_b}$ are exactly the following five, listed with their (unique) role
pairs:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{clll}
\toprule
 & vertices & edges (colors) & role pair \\
\midrule
(V1) & $0$ & --- & $\{p_0, v_0\}$ \\
(V2) & $0,\ b-1$ & $\mathrm V\{0,b{-}1\}$ & $\{p_0, p_1\}$ \\
(V3) & $0,\ 1$ & $\mathrm P\{0,1\}$ & $\{v_0, v_1\}$ \\
(V4) & $b-1,\ 0,\ 1$ & $\mathrm V\{0,b{-}1\},\ \mathrm P\{0,1\}$ & $\{p_1, v_1\}$ \\
(V5) & $b-1,\ 2,\ 1$ & $\mathrm V\{2,b{-}1\},\ \mathrm P\{1,2\}$ & $\{p_1, v_1\}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In particular: the role pairs $\{p_0, v_1\}$ and $\{v_0, p_1\}$ admit no visit;
the only visit for $\{p_0, p_1\}$ is (V2); the only visit for $\{v_0, v_1\}$ is
(V3); and $M(T_b) = 3$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
That each listed visit is admissible is a direct check from
Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory}. (V1): vertex $0$ carries $p_0$ and $v_0$ with different
port colors. (V2): first $=$ last edge $\mathrm V \ne \pc(p_0) = \pc(p_1) =
\mathrm P$. (V3): first $=$ last edge $\mathrm P \ne \pc(v_0) = \pc(v_1) =
\mathrm V$. (V4) and (V5): first edge $\mathrm V \ne \pc(p_1)$, last edge
$\mathrm P \ne \pc(v_1)$, and the middle vertex makes the colors alternate. The role
pairs listed are the only possible ones: e.g.\ in (V2) the first edge is
$\mathrm V$, so the role at $0$ must have port color $\ne \mathrm V$, i.e.\ $p_0$;
the same one-line check determines the role pair of each visit.

For completeness we must show no other admissible visit exists. Type-(ii) visits
need a vertex carrying two roles of different port colors; by
Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory} the only vertex carrying two roles is $0 = p_0 = v_0$,
giving (V1). A type-(i) visit starts at a terminal vertex with a constrained first
color (Lemma~\ref{lem:reversal}(b)). The terminal vertices are $0, 1, b-1$, and the
roles they carry leave exactly four possible (start vertex, first color)
\emph{stems}:
\[
\text{(A) } (0, \mathrm V) \text{ [role } p_0\text{]}, \quad
\text{(B) } (0, \mathrm P) \text{ [role } v_0\text{]}, \quad
\text{(C) } (1, \mathrm P) \text{ [role } v_1\text{]}, \quad
\text{(D) } (b-1, \mathrm V) \text{ [role } p_1\text{]}.
\]
Every admissible visit, read from its own first vertex, extends one of these stems,
so it suffices to enumerate, for each stem, all PC continuations, and to record those
moments at which the path stands at a terminal vertex with a last-edge color
admissible for a role there. Throughout we use the \emph{forcing facts} from
Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory}:
(F1) vertex $0$ has exactly one P-edge ($\{0,1\}$) and one V-edge ($\{0,b-1\}$);
(F2) vertex $1$ has exactly one V-edge ($\{1,b-2\}$);
(F3) vertex $b-1$ has exactly one P-edge ($\{b-2,b-1\}$);
and the segment behavior of Lemma~\ref{lem:segment}. Recall that no vertex of $S$ is
a terminal, so a path currently stranded in $S$ can never end an admissible visit
there.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stem (A): start $0$, first edge $\mathrm V$.}
By (F1) the first edge is $\mathrm V\{0, b-1\}$; the path stands at $b-1$ with last
color $\mathrm V$. \emph{Record:} role $p_1$ at $b-1$ has $\pc = \mathrm P \ne
\mathrm V$, so $(0, b-1)$ is admissible: this is (V2). To continue, the next edge
must be $\ne \mathrm V$, hence by (F3) it is $\mathrm P\{b-2,b-1\}$; the path enters
$S$ at $b-2$ with entry color $\mathrm P$ and $S$ untouched. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:segment}(b) there are two ways out of $S$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Immediate exit at $b-2$} by $\mathrm V\{1, b-2\}$: the path stands at
$1$ with last color $\mathrm V$. The only role at $1$ is $v_1$ with
$\pc = \mathrm V$: \emph{not} admissible. Continuing from $1$: next $\ne \mathrm V$,
so a P-edge: $\{0,1\}$ is excluded ($0$ visited), leaving $\mathrm P\{1,2\}$ into
$S$ at $2$. From $2$, next $\ne \mathrm P$: $\mathrm V\{2,b-1\}$ is excluded ($b-1$
visited), leaving the V-side of $\{2,3\}$. From $3$ onward the path is inside $S$
and moves monotonically upward (Lemma~\ref{lem:segment}(a)); it can never exit,
because the only exit vertices are $2$ (behind it) and $b-2$ (already visited), so
it strands in $S$. No admissible visit arises.
\item \emph{Full crossing to $2$}: the path arrives at $2$ by an internal edge of
color $\mathrm P$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:segment}(b) with $c = \mathrm P$), and any exit
edge must have color $\mathrm V$. The only V-colored external edge at $2$ is
$\{2, b-1\}$, whose endpoint $b-1$ is visited. The path strands in $S$ (vertex $2$
is in $S$); no admissible visit arises.
\end{itemize}
\emph{Stem (A) yields exactly (V2).}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stem (B): start $0$, first edge $\mathrm P$.}
By (F1) the first edge is $\mathrm P\{0,1\}$; the path stands at $1$ with last color
$\mathrm P$. \emph{Record:} role $v_1$ at $1$ has $\pc = \mathrm V \ne \mathrm P$:
admissible: this is (V3). To continue: next $\ne \mathrm P$, so by (F2) it is
$\mathrm V\{1, b-2\}$; the path enters $S$ at $b-2$ with entry color $\mathrm V$,
$S$ untouched. By Lemma~\ref{lem:segment}(b):
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Immediate exit at $b-2$} by $\mathrm P\{b-2, b-1\}$: the path stands at
$b-1$ with last color $\mathrm P$. The only role at $b-1$ is $p_1$ with
$\pc = \mathrm P$: not admissible. Continuing from $b-1$: next $\ne \mathrm P$, so a
V-edge: $\{0,b-1\}$ is excluded ($0$ visited), leaving $\mathrm V\{2,b-1\}$ into $S$
at $2$. From $2$, next $\ne \mathrm V$: $\mathrm P\{1,2\}$ is excluded ($1$
visited), leaving the P-side of $\{2,3\}$; the path moves monotonically upward
inside $S$ and strands ($b-2$ visited). No admissible visit.
\item \emph{Full crossing to $2$}: arrival color $\mathrm V$, so any exit edge must
be $\mathrm P$; the only P-colored external edge at $2$ is $\{1,2\}$, and $1$ is
visited. Strands. No admissible visit.
\end{itemize}
\emph{Stem (B) yields exactly (V3).}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stem (C): start $1$, first edge $\mathrm P$.}
The P-edges at $1$ are $\{0,1\}$ and $\{1,2\}$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory}); two
branches.
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Branch $1 \to 0$.} At $0$, last color $\mathrm P$. \emph{Record:} roles
at $0$: $p_0$ ($\pc = \mathrm P$, fails), $v_0$ ($\pc = \mathrm V$, admissible):
this is (V3) reversed. Continue: next $\ne \mathrm P$, by (F1) it is
$\mathrm V\{0, b-1\}$; at $b-1$, last color $\mathrm V$. \emph{Record:} role $p_1$:
admissible: this is (V4) reversed. Continue: next $\ne \mathrm V$, by (F3)
$\mathrm P\{b-2,b-1\}$ into $S$ at $b-2$, entry color $\mathrm P$, $S$ untouched.
Immediate exit by $\mathrm V\{1,b-2\}$: $1$ visited. Full crossing: arrival at $2$
with color $\mathrm P$, exit must be $\mathrm V$: only $\{2,b-1\}$, visited.
Strands; branch closed.
\item \emph{Branch $1 \to 2$.} The path enters $S$ at $2$ by $\mathrm P\{1,2\}$,
entry color $\mathrm P$, $S$ untouched. Immediate exit by $\mathrm V\{2, b-1\}$: at
$b-1$, last color $\mathrm V$. \emph{Record:} role $p_1$: admissible: this is (V5)
reversed. Continue from $b-1$: next $\ne \mathrm V$, by (F3) $\mathrm P\{b-2,b-1\}$
into $S$ at $b-2$ --- but now $S$ is touched (vertex $2$): from $b-2$ the
non-$\mathrm P$ continuations are $\mathrm V\{1,b-2\}$ ($1$ visited) and the V-side
of $\{b-3,b-2\}$, after which the path moves monotonically down inside $S$ and
strands at $3$ ($2$ visited). Branch closed. Alternatively, instead of exiting $S$
immediately at $2$, the path may cross: full crossing $2 \to b-2$ with entry color
$\mathrm P$ arrives at $b-2$ by color $\mathrm P$; exit must be $\mathrm V$: only
$\{1, b-2\}$, and $1$ is visited. Strands; branch closed.
\end{itemize}
\emph{Stem (C) yields exactly (V3), (V4), (V5) (reversed).}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stem (D): start $b-1$, first edge $\mathrm V$.}
The V-edges at $b-1$ are $\{0, b-1\}$ and $\{2, b-1\}$; two branches.
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Branch $b-1 \to 0$.} At $0$, last color $\mathrm V$. \emph{Record:}
roles at $0$: $v_0$ fails, $p_0$ admissible: this is (V2) reversed. Continue: next
$\ne \mathrm V$, by (F1) $\mathrm P\{0,1\}$; at $1$, last color $\mathrm P$.
\emph{Record:} role $v_1$: admissible: this is (V4). Continue: next $\ne \mathrm P$,
by (F2) $\mathrm V\{1,b-2\}$ into $S$ at $b-2$, entry color $\mathrm V$, $S$
untouched. Immediate exit by $\mathrm P\{b-2,b-1\}$: $b-1$ visited. Full crossing:
arrival at $2$ with color $\mathrm V$, exit must be $\mathrm P$: only $\{1,2\}$, and
$1$ is visited. Strands; branch closed.
\item \emph{Branch $b-1 \to 2$.} The path enters $S$ at $2$ by
$\mathrm V\{2,b-1\}$, entry color $\mathrm V$, $S$ untouched. Immediate exit by
$\mathrm P\{1,2\}$: at $1$, last color $\mathrm P$. \emph{Record:} role $v_1$:
admissible: this is (V5). Continue from $1$: next $\ne \mathrm P$, by (F2)
$\mathrm V\{1,b-2\}$ into $S$ at $b-2$ --- $S$ touched (vertex $2$): from $b-2$ the
non-$\mathrm V$ continuations are $\mathrm P\{b-2,b-1\}$ ($b-1$ visited) and the
P-side of $\{b-3,b-2\}$, then monotone descent stranding at $3$ ($2$ visited).
Branch closed. Alternatively, full crossing $2 \to b-2$ with entry color $\mathrm V$
arrives by color $\mathrm V$; exit must be $\mathrm P$: only $\{b-2,b-1\}$, and
$b-1$ is visited. Strands; branch closed.
\end{itemize}
\emph{Stem (D) yields exactly (V2), (V4), (V5).}

\medskip
Collecting the four stems: every admissible visit is one of (V1)--(V5) up to
reversal, and each is realized only with the listed role pair (checked at each
\emph{Record} step). In particular no admissible visit realizes $\{p_0, v_1\}$ or
$\{v_0, p_1\}$, and the maximum size is $3$, attained by (V4) and (V5).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:M3}
$M(T_b) = 3$ for every even $b \ge 6$, and $c^* := \liminf_n \pmin(n)/n$ satisfies
$c^* \le 6/b$ for every even $b \ge 6$; hence $c^* = 0$ and the displayed question
of Section~\ref{sec:intro} has a negative answer.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$M(T_b) = 3$ is part of Theorem~\ref{thm:classification}. For the second claim, take
$\pi_i = T_b$ for all $i$ in Theorem~\ref{thm:inflation} and let $a \to \infty$:
$\pmin(ab)/(ab) \le \bigl((2a-3)\cdot 3 + 2b\bigr)/(ab) \to 6/b$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The parity is essential]\label{rem:parity}
For \emph{odd} $b$, $b - 4$ is odd and the last internal edge of a full crossing has
color $\bar c$, so the exit edge may have color $c$. The route
$0 \xrightarrow{\mathrm P} 1 \xrightarrow{\mathrm V} b-2 \rightsquigarrow 2
\xrightarrow{\mathrm V\{2,b-1\}} b-1$ (full crossing in the middle) then becomes
properly colored and is an admissible visit for $\{v_0, p_1\}$ of size $b$. Exact
computation (Section~\ref{sec:comp}) confirms $M(T_b) = b$ for odd
$b \in \{7, 9, 11, 13\}$ and $M(T_b) = 3$ for even $6 \le b \le 100$, and confirms
the \emph{exact list} of Theorem~\ref{thm:classification} for even
$6 \le b \le 14$ by independent brute-force enumeration of all admissible visits
(ancillary file \texttt{verify\_M.py}).
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Two visits to one $T_b$-block]\label{cor:pairs}
Let $\sigma = \tau[\pi_1, \dots, \pi_a]$ be an inflation, let $B_i$ be a block whose
pattern $\pi_i$ equals $T_b$ for some even $b \ge 6$, and let $Q$ be a PC path in
$H_\sigma$. Then the interior visits of $Q$ to $B_i$ have total size at most $4$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:crosscount}, the block has at most four incident cross edges, one
per role; each interior visit uses two of them (its entry and its exit, distinct
edges), and every cross edge is used by $Q$ at most once. Hence there are at most two
interior visits; if there is at most one, its size is at most $M(T_b) = 3 \le 4$. If
there are two, they use all four cross edges, so their role pairs partition
$\{p_0, p_1, v_0, v_1\}$, and the two visits are vertex-disjoint (disjoint runs of
the simple path $Q$). By the classification
(Theorem~\ref{thm:classification}) the three possible partitions give:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\{p_0, p_1\} \,/\, \{v_0, v_1\}$: the only visits are (V2) $= (0, b-1)$ and
(V3) $= (0,1)$, which share vertex $0$ --- impossible;
\item $\{p_0, v_1\} \,/\, \{v_0, p_1\}$: no visits exist for either pair ---
impossible;
\item $\{p_0, v_0\} \,/\, \{p_1, v_1\}$: sizes $1$ (V1) and at most $3$ (V4 or V5):
total at most $4$.
\end{itemize}
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Main Theorem}\label{sec:main}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] For every even $b \ge 6$ and $a \ge 2$,
$\ \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \le 6a + 2b$, where
$\sigma_{a,b} = \mathrm{id}_a[T_b, \dots, T_b]$, i.e.\
$\sigma_{a,b}(jb+r) = jb + T_b(r)$.
\item[(b)] For every $n \ge 100$, $\ \pmin(n) \le 10\sqrt n + 10$.
\end{enumerate}
In particular $\pmin(n) = O(\sqrt n)$ and $\pmin(n)/n \to 0$, using only
$M(T_b) = 3$ and the Inflation Lemma.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(a) Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:inflation} with $M(\pi_i) = 3$ and $b_i = b$:
$\rho \le (2a-3)\cdot 3 + 2b \le 6a + 2b$.

(b) Let $b = 2\lfloor \sqrt n / 2 \rfloor$, so $b$ is even, $\sqrt n - 2 < b \le
\sqrt n$, and $b \ge 10$ since $n \ge 100$. Let $a = \lfloor n/b \rfloor \ge 2$ and
$s = n - (a-1)b$, so $b \le s < 2b$. Define $\sigma \in S_n$ as the inflation (any
outer permutation, say the identity) of the following list of blocks:
\begin{itemize}
\item if $s$ is even: $a - 1$ copies of $T_b$ and one copy of $T_s$
(valid: $s$ even, $s \ge b \ge 10$);
\item if $s$ is odd: $a - 1$ copies of $T_b$, one copy of $T_{s-3}$, and one block
of size $3$ with the identity pattern $\iota_3 = (0,1,2)$ (valid: $s - 3$ is even
and $s - 3 \ge b - 2 \ge 8$, since $s \ge b$ with $s$ odd and $b$ even force
$s \ge b + 1$).
\end{itemize}
Every block has transit number at most $3$: the $T$-blocks by
Theorem~\ref{thm:classification}, the single $\iota_3$-block trivially (it has only
$3$ vertices). The number of blocks is $m \le a + 1 \le n/b + 1$ and the largest
block has size at most $2b - 1$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:inflation},
\[
\pmin(n) \;\le\; (2m - 3)\cdot 3 + 2(2b - 1)
\;\le\; \frac{6n}{b} + 4b - 5 .
\]
Since $b \ge \sqrt n - 2$ and $\sqrt n \ge 10$,
\[
\frac{6n}{b} \;\le\; \frac{6n}{\sqrt n - 2}
\;=\; 6\sqrt n + \frac{12\sqrt n}{\sqrt n - 2}
\;\le\; 6\sqrt n + 15,
\]
using $\sqrt n/(\sqrt n - 2) \le 10/8$ for $\sqrt n \ge 10$. With $4b \le 4\sqrt n$
we get $\pmin(n) \le 10\sqrt n + 10$.
\end{proof}

The refined constants use the classification once more, through
Corollary~\ref{cor:pairs}.

\begin{proposition}[Refined constants]\label{prop:refined}
For every even $b \ge 6$ and $a \ge 2$, $\ \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \le 4a + 2b$; and for
every $n \ge 100$, $\ \pmin(n) \le 8\sqrt n + 20$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First claim: let $Q$ be a PC path in $H_{\sigma_{a,b}}$ with $q$ visits. If $q = 1$
then $|Q| \le b$. Otherwise, by Lemma~\ref{lem:visits} the first and last visits have
at most $b$ vertices each, and by Corollary~\ref{cor:pairs} the interior visits
contribute at most $4$ per block, i.e.\ at most $4a$ in total. Hence
$\rho \le 4a + 2b$.

Second claim: take the construction from the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}(b).
Interior visits contribute at most $4$ per $T$-block (Corollary~\ref{cor:pairs};
the corollary applies verbatim to $T_b$, $T_s$, and $T_{s-3}$) and at most
$2 \cdot 3 = 6$ in total for the single $\iota_3$-block (at most two interior
visits --- Lemma~\ref{lem:crosscount} --- of size at most $3$ each). End visits
contribute at most $2(2b - 1)$. Hence
\[
\pmin(n) \;\le\; 4\Bigl(\frac n b + 1\Bigr) + 6 + 2(2b-1)
\;=\; \frac{4n}{b} + 4b + 8
\;\le\; \bigl(4\sqrt n + 10\bigr) + 4\sqrt n + 8
\;\le\; 8\sqrt n + 20,
\]
using $4n/(\sqrt n - 2) = 4\sqrt n + 8\sqrt n/(\sqrt n - 2) \le 4\sqrt n + 10$ for
$\sqrt n \ge 10$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Explicit long path: the family is $\Theta(a+b)$]
\label{prop:lower}
For every even $b \ge 6$ and $a \ge 2$, $\ \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \ge 2a + 2b - 7$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $(j, \ell)$ for the vertex at position $jb + \ell$ ($0 \le j < a$,
$0 \le \ell < b$), so block $j$ is a copy of $H_{T_b}$ on $\{(j,0), \dots,
(j,b-1)\}$, and the values are: $(j,0) \mapsto jb$, $(j,1) \mapsto jb + b - 1$,
$(j,\ell) \mapsto jb + \ell$ for $2 \le \ell \le b-2$, and
$(j, b-1) \mapsto jb + 1$. Besides the internal edges (Lemma~\ref{lem:inventory} in
each block), the cross edges are: the P-edges $\{(j, b-1), (j+1, 0)\}$ (consecutive
positions) and the V-edges $\{(j, 1), (j+1, 0)\}$ (values $jb + b - 1$ and
$(j+1)b$). Within block $j$, note the V-edge $\{(j,0), (j,b-1)\}$ (values $jb$ and
$jb+1$) and the V-edge $\{(j,2), (j,b-1)\}$ (values $jb+2$ and $jb+1$).

The following walk is a PC path; colors are indicated over each arrow, and each edge
exists by the inventory just listed.
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Stage 1 (block $0$).} Start at $(0, b-2)$ and descend the segment to
$(0, 2)$ along the doubled run, choosing colors
$\mathrm V, \mathrm P, \mathrm V, \dots$ alternately; this uses $b - 4$ edges, and
since $b - 4$ is even the last of them (into $(0,2)$) has color $\mathrm P$. Then
take $\mathrm V\,\{(0,2), (0,b-1)\}$, then the P-cross edge to $(1, 0)$. Colors:
$\dots, \mathrm P, \mathrm V, \mathrm P$ --- properly colored.
\item \emph{Stage 2 (blocks $1, \dots, a-2$).} From $(j, 0)$ (just entered by a
P-cross edge) take $\mathrm V\,\{(j,0), (j,b-1)\}$, then the P-cross edge to
$(j+1, 0)$; repeat. Colors alternate $\mathrm P, \mathrm V, \mathrm P, \dots$
throughout.
\item \emph{Stage 3 (block $a-1$).} From $(a-1, 0)$ (entered by a P-cross edge)
take $\mathrm V\,\{(a-1,0), (a-1,b-1)\}$, then $\mathrm P\,\{(a-1,b-2),
(a-1,b-1)\}$, then descend the segment from $(a-1, b-2)$ to $(a-1, 2)$ choosing
colors $\mathrm V, \mathrm P, \dots$ alternately, and stop at $(a-1, 2)$.
\end{itemize}
All vertices are distinct (stages use disjoint blocks; within each stage the listed
vertices are distinct), and consecutive edge colors differ at every junction as
indicated. The vertex count is $(b - 2)$ in stage 1 ($b-3$ segment vertices plus
$(0, b-1)$), $2(a-2)$ in stage 2, and $(b - 1)$ in stage 3 ($(a-1,0)$,
$(a-1,b-1)$, and the $b-3$ segment vertices), for a total of $2a + 2b - 7$. The
construction is verified mechanically, edge by edge, in the ancillary file
\texttt{verify\_path.py}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Constants; exact values]\label{rem:constants}
Together, Propositions~\ref{prop:refined} and~\ref{prop:lower} give
$2(a+b) - 7 \le \rho(\sigma_{a,b}) \le 4a + 2b$: the family is $\Theta(a + b)$, and
with $a \asymp b$ it realizes $\Theta(\sqrt n)$, so the upper bound of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main-intro} is achieved by this family up to the constant. Exact
machine computation (Section~\ref{sec:comp}) gives $\rho(\sigma_{a,b}) = 2a + 2b -
4$ at every tested point up to $n = 720$; we conjecture this holds for all $a \ge
3$, even $b \ge 6$, but we neither prove nor use it. Optimizing the proven bounds
at $n = ab$: the upper bound $4a + 2b$ with $b \asymp \sqrt{2n}$ gives
$\pmin(n) \le 4\sqrt{2n} + O(1) \approx 5.66\sqrt n$ along such $n$.
\end{remark}

\section{Computational verification}\label{sec:comp}

None of the proofs above depend on computation. This section specifies the
computations that independently verify them, and the empirical sharpness of the
construction. \textbf{All code, command lines, and output logs are provided as
ancillary files with this submission} (directory \texttt{artifact/}; file
\texttt{README.md} there maps each claim below to a script and a log; a manifest
with SHA-256 checksums is included).

\paragraph{Algorithm.} The basic object is the exact longest-PC-path search. Its
state space is the set of triples (\emph{visited set}, \emph{current vertex},
\emph{last edge color}); the initial states are all $(\{x, w\}, w, c)$ over edges
$\{x, w\}$ of color $c$; a transition extends a state by an unvisited neighbor along
an edge of the other color. Every PC path corresponds to a unique state, so
$\rho(\sigma)$ is the maximum cardinality of the visited set over all
\emph{reachable} states, computed by an exhaustive search that visits every
reachable state exactly once (a memo set of states; no pruning of any kind). The
method is exact by construction; its cost is the number of reachable states. That
number is exponential in general --- on unstructured instances we used the method
only for $n \le 19$ --- but is small on the structured families here, because the
gadget strands most partial paths quickly: for $\sigma_{a,b}$ we measure
$57{,}066$ reachable states at $n = 80$, $5{,}012{,}186$ at $n = 600$, and
$7{,}273{,}586$ at $n = 720$ (the logs report the count for every table row). The constrained variants computing $M(\pi)$
(fixed start/end terminals and first/last colors) are the same search with the
initial and accepting states restricted.

\paragraph{Implementations.} Three independent implementations were used: a plain
recursive enumerator and a memoized iterative one (both Python, in the artifact),
which agree on $300$ random permutations of sizes $4$--$8$; and a separately
written C++ solver (also in the artifact) from a parallel verification effort,
which agrees with the Python solvers on every instance checked by both, including
exact values of $\rho(\sigma_{a,b})$ at $(a,b) \in \{(6,8), (5,10), (4,12)\}$ and
the trap-family values of Appendix~\ref{app:trap} at six points. In addition, the
artifact contains a \emph{minimal independent verifier}
(\texttt{verify\_M.py}, under $100$ lines, written directly from
Definition~\ref{def:M} with no shared code), which re-derives $M(T_b)$ and the
complete list of admissible visits by brute force for $b \le 14$.

\paragraph{Verified statements.} Each item states the claim, its range, and its log.

\section{Lower bounds and open problems}\label{sec:disc}

Following \cite{Liz26}, a \emph{$\pm1$-block} of $\sigma$ is a maximal interval of
positions on which consecutive values differ by a fixed
$\varepsilon \in \{+1,-1\}$ (singletons allowed); let $b(\sigma)$ be their number
and $s_{\max}(\sigma)$ the largest block size. Two elementary facts frame the
lower-bound question. First, the $\pm1$-blocks partition the positions, so
\[
s_{\max}(\sigma) \cdot b(\sigma) \;\ge\; n,
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad
\max\bigl(s_{\max}(\sigma),\, b(\sigma)\bigr) \;\ge\; \sqrt n .
\]
Second, $\rho(\sigma) \ge s_{\max}(\sigma)$ always (traverse a largest block along
its doubled run; \cite{Liz26}). Consequently, if there were an absolute $c > 0$
with $\rho(\sigma) \ge c\, b(\sigma)$ for all $\sigma$, then
$\pmin(n) \ge c\sqrt n$ would follow, and with Theorem~\ref{thm:main}:

\begin{conjecture}\label{conj:sqrt}
$\pmin(n) = \Theta(\sqrt n)$.
\end{conjecture}

We emphasize the asymmetry of the evidence: the upper bound is proved, while for
the lower bound we have only the following partial information about $\rho$ versus
$b(\sigma)$, which we believe is of independent interest.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Exhaustively for $n \le 12$:} $\rho(\sigma) \ge b(\sigma)$ for every
$\sigma \in S_n$ (machine-verified over all $479{,}001{,}600$ permutations at
$n = 12$; artifact logs \texttt{exh7.txt}--\texttt{exh12.txt}; equality occurs).
\item \textbf{Asymptotically the constant $1$ fails:} each copy of $T_b$
contributes four $\pm1$-blocks ($\{0\}$, $\{1\}$, $[2, b-2]$, $\{b-1\}$), so
$b(\sigma_{a,b}) = 4a$, while exact machine computation
(\texttt{bigtable.log}) gives $\rho(\sigma_{20,12}) = 60 < 80 =
b(\sigma_{20,12})$ at $n = 240$, and similarly $76 < 96$ at $(24,16)$ and
$96 < 120$ at $(30,20)$. These are finite, reproducible counterexamples to
$\rho \ge b(\sigma)$.
\item \textbf{Proven window:} by \eqref{eq:twosided},
$\tfrac{\rho(\sigma_{a,b})}{b(\sigma_{a,b})} \in
\bigl[\tfrac12 + \tfrac{2b-7}{4a},\ 1 + \tfrac{b}{2a}\bigr]$; the conjectured
exact formula $2a + 2b - 4$ would give limit $\tfrac12$ as $a/b \to \infty$. We
know of no $\sigma$ with $\rho(\sigma) < \tfrac12 b(\sigma)$.
\end{itemize}
Thus the sharp form of the lower-bound question is whether
$\rho \ge c\,b(\sigma)$ holds for some $c \le \tfrac12$; any such $c$ settles
Conjecture~\ref{conj:sqrt}. At the opposite extreme, even $\pmin(n) \to \infty$
appears to be open: the bound $\rho \ge s_{\max} + 1$ of \cite{Liz26} degenerates
for permutations with all blocks of bounded size, and our methods produce no lower
bounds at all. (For the special case $b(\sigma) = n$ --- no two consecutive
positions carrying consecutive values --- exhaustive computation for $n \le 12$
found every such permutation to be PC-Hamiltonian, $\rho = n$; this extreme case
is wide open.)

On the gadget side, the exhaustive minimum $\min_{\pi \in S_b} M(\pi) = 3$ for
$4 \le b \le 9$ hints that transit numbers cannot be made arbitrarily small;
deciding whether $M(\pi) \ge 3$ for all $|\pi| \ge 4$, or determining the true
minimum growth of $M$ as a function of $b$, would calibrate how far inflation
alone can go. (Heuristically it cannot beat $\sqrt n$ in any case: the end-visit
term ties the bound to the block size, and nesting inflations doubles the
visit-count bound per level.)

Two further directions. First, the analogous question for the union of two
Hamiltonian \emph{cycles}: we expect the method to adapt --- the port calculus
acquires two wraparound cross edges and the visit count rises accordingly --- but
we have not carried out the analysis and do not claim the result. Second, the
Lov\'asz-side question (Section~\ref{subsec:scope}): for the graphs $G(n, \sigma)$
of \cite{Liz26} built from $\sigma_{a,b}$ or from the chains of
Appendix~\ref{app:trap}, is the maximum number of matching edges on a path still
$\Theta(n)$? The properly-colored relaxation is now known to be lossy; quantifying
the loss on these explicit instances may indicate whether the matching-edge
parameter $f(n)$ is linear after all.

\section{A second construction: trap chains}\label{app:trap}

This appendix gives an independent proof of $\pmin(n) = O(\sqrt n)$ by a different
family, found in a parallel verification effort. It does not use
Sections~\ref{sec:ports}--\ref{sec:gadget}; conversely, the main text does not use
this appendix. The mechanism is again a parity obstruction, but localized
differently: here \emph{large reversed blocks of odd size, flanked by singletons},
are traps that no properly colored path can cross.

\begin{definition}\label{def:chain}
Let $C = (c_0, c_1, \dots, c_r)$ be a composition of $n$ with $c_t \ge 1$ and
\emph{no two consecutive entries equal to $1$}. Put $P_t = c_0 + \dots + c_{t-1}$,
and let block $t$ occupy positions and values $[P_t, P_t + c_t - 1]$, reversed:
\[
\sigma_C(P_t + i) \;=\; P_t + c_t - 1 - i, \qquad 0 \le i < c_t .
\]
Write $L_t = P_t$ and $R_t = P_t + c_t - 1$ (the \emph{ports} of block $t$).
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:chain-inv}
$H_{\sigma_C}$ consists exactly of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] doubled pairs $\{p, p+1\}$ for all consecutive positions inside each
block;
\item[(b)] for each $0 \le t < r$, one P-only cross edge $\{R_t, L_{t+1}\}$;
\item[(c)] for each $0 \le t < r$, one V-only cross edge $\{L_t, R_{t+1}\}$;
\item[(d)] nothing else.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, the cross edges incident to block $t$ attach as follows
(\emph{port--color crossover}): at $L_t$, a P-edge toward block $t-1$ and a V-edge
toward block $t+1$; at $R_t$, a V-edge toward block $t-1$ and a P-edge toward
block $t+1$ (omitting nonexistent neighbors). Non-port vertices carry no cross
edges.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(a) Inside a block, consecutive positions hold consecutive (descending) values:
both a P-edge and a V-edge. (b) The positions $R_t, L_{t+1}$ are consecutive;
their values are $P_t$ (block minimum) and $P_{t+1} + c_{t+1} - 1$ (block
maximum), which differ by $c_t + c_{t+1} - 1 \ge 2$ since not both $c_t, c_{t+1}$
equal $1$: P-only. (c) The values $P_t + c_t - 1$ (maximum of block $t$, held at
position $L_t$ because the block descends) and $P_t + c_t = P_{t+1}$ (minimum of
block $t+1$, held at $R_{t+1}$) are consecutive; the positions $L_t, R_{t+1}$
differ by $c_t + c_{t+1} - 1 \ge 2$: V-only. (d) Positions in non-consecutive
blocks are never adjacent; values in non-consecutive blocks never differ by $1$
(value ranges are contiguous in block order); and within consecutive blocks,
(b)/(c) list the only position-adjacent (resp.\ value-adjacent) pairs not internal
to a block. The crossover statement reads off (b) and (c) at blocks $t-1, t, t+1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:chain-visit}
Let $Q$ be a PC path in $H_{\sigma_C}$ and let $t$ be a block containing no
endpoint of $Q$. Then every maximal run of consecutive $Q$-vertices inside block
$t$ is either (i) a single port vertex, or (ii) a \emph{full traversal} of all
$c_t$ vertices from one port to the other. In case (ii), if the entering cross
edge has color $\chi$, the exiting cross edge has color $\ne \chi$ if and only if
$c_t$ is odd.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
A run with no endpoint of $Q$ has an entering and an exiting cross edge, which by
Lemma~\ref{lem:chain-inv} attach only at ports; the run's internal edges lie in
the block, whose internal graph is a path (with doubled pairs). If the run enters
and leaves at the same port it is that single vertex (re-reaching the entry port
would repeat it); the two cross edges at one port have different colors
(Lemma~\ref{lem:chain-inv}), as the PC condition requires. Otherwise it goes from
one port to the other through the entire internal path: a full traversal, with
$c_t - 1$ internal edges. The PC condition forces the internal colors to alternate
starting with $\bar\chi$, so the last internal edge has color $\chi$ iff
$c_t - 1$ is even; the exit edge differs from the last internal edge, so it has
color $\bar\chi$ iff $c_t$ is odd.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Trap Lemma]\label{lem:trap}
Let block $t$ of $\sigma_C$ have odd size $c_t \ge 3$ and suppose its neighbors
are singleton blocks: $c_{t-1} = c_{t+1} = 1$ (with $1 \le t \le r-1$). Let $Q$ be
a PC path with no endpoint in block $t$. Then $Q$ uses at most $2$ vertices of
block $t$ (at most one visit per port, and no full traversal).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $A$ for block $t$ and let $s^-, s^+$ be the (single) vertices of blocks
$t-1$, $t+1$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:chain-inv} the four cross edges at $A$ are
\[
\text{at } L_A:\ \{s^-, L_A\} \text{ (P)}, \quad \{L_A, s^+\} \text{ (V)};
\qquad
\text{at } R_A:\ \{s^-, R_A\} \text{ (V)}, \quad \{R_A, s^+\} \text{ (P)}.
\]
Suppose $Q$ makes a full traversal of $A$, entering by a cross edge of color
$\chi$; since $c_t$ is odd, it exits by a cross edge of color $\bar\chi$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:chain-visit}). Four cases:
\begin{enumerate}
\item enter at $L_A$ by $\{s^-, L_A\}$ ($\chi = \mathrm P$): the exit is the
V-colored cross edge at $R_A$, namely $\{s^-, R_A\}$ --- but $s^-$ was the vertex
visited immediately before entering, a repetition;
\item enter at $L_A$ by $\{L_A, s^+\}$ ($\chi = \mathrm V$): the exit is the
P-edge $\{R_A, s^+\}$ --- repeats $s^+$;
\item enter at $R_A$ by $\{s^-, R_A\}$ ($\chi = \mathrm V$): the exit is the
P-edge $\{s^-, L_A\}$ --- repeats $s^-$;
\item enter at $R_A$ by $\{R_A, s^+\}$ ($\chi = \mathrm P$): the exit is the
V-edge $\{L_A, s^+\}$ --- repeats $s^+$.
\end{enumerate}
Each case contradicts simplicity of $Q$. So every visit is a single port vertex;
there are two ports.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:trap-main}
Let $L \ge 3$ be odd, $k \ge 1$, $0 \le p \le L+1$, and
$C = (2, (L, 1)^k, 3+p, 2)$, a composition of $n = (L+1)k + p + 7$ with no two
consecutive $1$'s. Then
\[
\rho(\sigma_C) \;\le\; 3k + 3L + p + 1 .
\]
Consequently $\pmin(n) \le 7\sqrt n + O(1)$ for all sufficiently large $n$, by
choosing $L$ the largest odd integer $\le \sqrt n$ and
$k = \lfloor (n-7)/(L+1) \rfloor$, $p = n - 7 - (L+1)k$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The blocks, in order, are: a $2$-block; $A_1, s_1, A_2, s_2, \dots, A_k, s_k$
where each $A_t$ has size $L$ and each $s_t$ is a singleton; a $(3+p)$-block; a
$2$-block. For $2 \le t \le k$ the block $A_t$ is flanked by the singletons
$s_{t-1}$ and $s_t$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:trap} applies to it; $A_1$ is flanked on
the left by the initial $2$-block and is not trapped.

Fix a PC path $Q$ and charge each block with the number of $Q$-vertices it
contains; $\rho(Q)$ is the sum of the charges. We bound the charges by category:
\begin{itemize}
\item each singleton $s_t$: charge $\le 1$ (its size); total $\le k$;
\item each trapped block $A_t$ ($2 \le t \le k$) containing no endpoint of $Q$:
charge $\le 2$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:trap}); there are at least $(k-1) - 2$ such blocks,
and at most $2$ trapped blocks may contain an endpoint of $Q$, each with charge
$\le L$ (its size). Hence the trapped blocks contribute at most
$2(k-1) + 2(L - 2)$ in total (each endpoint block exceeds the trap bound $2$ by at
most $L - 2$);
\item $A_1$: charge $\le L$ (its size);
\item the three remaining blocks: charges $\le 2$, $\le 3+p$, $\le 2$ (their
sizes); total $\le 7 + p$.
\end{itemize}
Summing: $\rho(Q) \le k + 2(k-1) + 2(L-2) + L + (7+p) = 3k + 3L + p + 1$.
(If an endpoint of $Q$ lies in a non-trapped block, the corresponding excess term
is simply not needed; the bound only improves.)

For the corollary: with $L \le \sqrt n$ odd and maximal,
$k \le n/(L+1) \le n/L$ and $p \le L + 1$, so
$\rho \le 3n/L + 4L + 4 = 7\sqrt n + O(1)$ using $L = \sqrt n - O(1)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:trap-data}
The parity hypothesis is again essential: for even $L$,
Lemma~\ref{lem:chain-visit} makes a full traversal \emph{preserve} the boundary
color, the four cases of Lemma~\ref{lem:trap} become consistent, and computation
shows the analogous even-$L$ chains are fully traversable ($\rho$ grows with
slope $1$). Exact computation (artifact log \texttt{check\_trap.log}, plus the
parallel C++ solver) gives the exact values $\rho(\sigma_C) = 2k + 2(L-1)$ for
$C = (2,(L,1)^k,3,2)$, odd $L \in \{5,7,9,13,15,21\}$ and all $k \ge 2$ within
reach ($n \le 57$): the same per-period slope $2$ as the family of
Section~\ref{sec:main}. We do not know whether the two families are related beyond
sharing this slope.
\end{remark}
